CLAIMS 01Why this structured format is important & valuable

Knowing what's true is hard — and we're not getting better at it where it counts

  • Knowing what is true, reliable, and consistent is extraordinarily hard — Socrates showed it 2,500 years ago, and we are not significantly better at it than the Athenians were.
  • Most people cannot defend even their deepest beliefs for more than a few logical steps in either direction — toward justifications or toward consequences.
  • Most people cannot even state the serious alternatives to the positions they hold — and if you don't understand the other answers to a question, you don't really understand the question.
  • We advance fastest in domains that admit concrete refutation — where reality slaps down errors clearly, the way gravity corrects a toddler. Knowing when a thing is wrong is what lets us find what might be right.
  • That gives three fields of relatively reliable knowledge: technology (the first falsifiable field, so obvious it barely registered as "knowledge"), mathematics (the first and still most certain abstract knowledge — errors are easy to show, and we delight in showing them), and empirical science (a disciplined method for checking hypotheses against reality and guarding against wishful bias).
  • These three do not exhaust knowledge. The questions humans care about most — ethical, political, religious, social — are metaphysically intractable, too complex, or play out over timescales too long to test. They can't be proven or falsified, yet they govern what we should do.
  • Even math and science rest on webs of argument about meaning and interpretation that are not themselves proofs or experiments.
  • The stakes are rising: systems whose power exceeds their ability to control that power rarely last. In an era of nuclear weapons, climate change, AI, and cheap bioengineering, weak reasoning in the untestable domains is an existential liability, not a philosophical inconvenience.
  • We need a genuinely new advance in ways of knowing when arguments are false or weak even when they can't be decisively disproven.

Bare assertion is the soil propaganda grows in

  • When claims to truth are bare assertions rather than references to their full justifying arguments, the only limits on what can be effectively asserted are loudness, monopoly over the narrative, charisma, and button-pushing of our irrational minds.
  • Not having full chains of justification publicly comparable is what makes propaganda possible — whoever owns the loudest voice controls the perception of truth by drowning out the rest.
  • "Alternative facts" and "fake news" are symptoms of missing infrastructure, not just bad actors.

The structural thesis: a 2-D thing forced into 1-D strings

  • Speech is bound to time; time as we experience it is one-dimensional; so speech is one-dimensional — it can pick only a single path through the space of everything sayable, leaving every other path unsaid and unmarked.
  • Writing freed us from time, but out of habit we imported the old linear constraint into a medium that didn't require it. Sentences, paragraphs, pages, books are all single run-on strings — "one and only one of the infinite possible paths."
  • Ideas are not linear. They branch, fork, recombine, and interweave; one claim leads to and from many others. A faithful map of reasoning needs at least two dimensions.
  • Representing a branching web in 1-D strings is like a logic puzzle ("the woman in the purple shirt sits two seats left of Bob…") used to convey what a single picture shows at a glance — same information, but the linear form strips out the structure of the connections.
  • "Maps, to be useful, must reflect the territory faithfully." Missing this cost humanity thousands of years of avoidable ignorance.
  • No idea is an island: the provenance of a belief is as integral as the belief itself — without its arguments and evidence, all that exists is a bald claim with no ground for acceptance or rejection.

The next step in a long lineage

  • Our rise to dominance rests entirely on understanding the world — and that has grown with each leap in sharing and preserving information: language → writing → the printing press → the internet.
  • The needed next leap is not more information — we are drowning in it. The bottleneck is no longer scarcity but the chaotic overabundance of knowledge and the difficulty of organizing and assessing it.
  • Every field is a possibility space; progress is the gradual mapping, refining, and clarifying of the objects and relationships in it. Nothing yet shows the full map of what's been said, which ideas connect to which, what's settled, and where the edges now are.
  • The proposed leap: a way to map what is known — draw related ideas together, show each connection to its grounds and consequences, expose edges and gaps, and reliably assess the strength of every piece.
  • Stated as two goals: a direct one — collect and intuitively organize all of humanity's knowledge in one accessible place — and an indirect one — raise the cultural standard for what counts as an acceptably reasonable argument.

Wikipedia for arguments, not facts

  • Wikipedia is for facts: neutral-point-of-view, consensus description. Excellent for "where is Timbuktu." Optimized for description.
  • Reasonable is for arguments: the strongest case from every side of a disputed question, stated clearly and placed side by side with its assumptions, evidence, and consequences. Optimized for persuasion & comparison.
  • Cultural echo: Wikipedia + smartphones ended the "pub argument" over checkable facts and made it socially costly to be confidently wrong about a fact. An accessible public repository of the best arguments would make it costly to expound a weak belief — anyone who checks has the strongest rebuttal in seconds.
  • The aim is to raise the cultural floor for a reasonable argument and tighten the Overton window toward more defensible positions.
  • The explicit ambition: bring the clarity and precision of non-quantitative thought as near to mathematics as humanly possible, by reducing ideas to their smallest coherent components.
  • It follows Wikipedia's proven lesson: given a good format and space, people will volunteer high-quality work — and quality rises with the number and diversity of contributors.

CLAIMS 02How the site would arrange & visually convey knowledge

The Graph and its nodes

  • The central artifact is the Graph: a single, durable, continuously evolving, user-generated network of ideas, visually connected by strings and spatial position that represent their logical relationships.
  • It grows toward a complete-but-always-growing ideological taxonomy of the sum total of human knowledge — recording not merely what is believed, but the structured chains of argument and evidence that justify each belief.
  • An idea is a node: a single claim about the world, reduced as far as possible to its smallest coherent, meaningful component, so it can be examined for clarity, precision, and validity with minimum ambiguity.
  • Argument stacks: a hot question may draw thousands of comments; every distinct version of one single point collapses into a stack. The community floats the clearest, strongest phrasing to the top and sinks the murkiest — but nothing is deleted; weaker versions stay visible beneath. A chaotic question resolves into a handful of stacks, each a plausible answer showing its best face beside its strongest opposition.

Position is argument: the four relations

Ideas connect by four logical relationships, each given a fixed spatial meaning so that placement itself carries the argument:

Grounds — to the left

What justifies this idea: its supporting assumptions and evidence. Read justification by moving left.

Dependents — to the right

What follows from this idea: its consequences. Trace implications by moving right.

Antitheses — a ring up & down

What opposes or counters it: its strongest rebuttals, held in direct visual confrontation.

Alternative statements — a z-axis stack

The same idea re-expressed — different phrasings, framings, and explanatory levels at one point in the plane.

  • Users place ideas by drawing strings from the appropriate side to related nodes; placement is set by the cumulative weight of votes.
  • A node may have any number of each relation, but may not hold logically contradictory relations to the same neighbor (not both a Ground and a Consequence of X; not both an Antithesis and an Alternative of X).
  • When placement votes conflict, the majority placement becomes prominent — but minority placements persist, stay visible, and can grow into the new majority. Nothing true-seeming is ever permanently silenced; it is only ranked.

Two kinds of zoom

  • Zoom of scale. The macro view describes overall map structure and coherence and de-emphasizes individual connections; the micro view switches mode to show one node's immediate connections lucidly and comprehensively.
  • Zoom of explanation. Every concept can be explained at a sliding depth — ELI5 → 10 → 15 → 25 → PhD — so an amateur and an adept can stand on the same node.
  • Alternative-statement stacks also hold explanations pitched to different kinds of minds (e.g. mathematically- vs. literarily-inclined).

Structural affordances

  • Holes as well as nodes — explicit placeholders for things we'd like to know, so the map charts its own frontiers and invites strategies to reach them.
  • Recombining paths — two sibling nodes can share a child; reasoning converges, not just branches.
  • Conjunction — express that node1 + node2 together imply node3, distinct from either alone implying it.
  • Self-arranging physics — the canvas settles toward a lowest-energy layout: gravity-like attraction pulls related structure together while repulsion keeps individual nodes legibly apart.

Reading a node's health at a glance

  • Edge color (red → white): how reliable the rating is — how much weighted voting it has received.
  • Brightness: Reasonableness. Ideas that sink below threshold fade to deep gray — "tried and found wanting," still visible, never erased.
  • Body color (dark gray → white): Lucidity / clarity.
  • Measures stay neutral gray until enough votes reach their probable stability point.
  • Net effect: bright/white/well-rated = strong, clear, settled; dim/dark/red = where new and better ideas are needed. The map doubles as a to-do list for editors.

Further views & modes

  • Global-brain view: activity ebbs and flows across the graph like electrochemical signals between neurons (aesthetic).
  • History / biography mode: rotate the graph so its long axis runs through time; position people, organizations, and events over a background world map.
  • Personal belief structures: map your own beliefs onto the Graph and be notified when a load-bearing node beneath them is updated or challenged. The system pinpoints exactly where, in a disagreement, your reasoning parted from someone else's — the last place you agreed, and therefore the last place worth arguing.
  • General-purpose substrate: the same branching-graph software could map history's causal/temporal relations, a full taxonomy of biology, or the collaborative paragraph-by-paragraph / shot-by-shot / element-by-element construction of stories, films, and other works.

CLAIMS 03Mechanics to surface reasonable claims & resist gaming, trolling, disinformation

The problem being solved

  • Open forums and comment sections reliably degenerate into cesspits; online anonymity breeds hostility that derails productive discussion.
  • A site for crowd-sourced reasonable argument must therefore be especially active in preventing trolls, malice, and idiocy from derailing useful conversation.

The reputation-weighted loop

  • Accounts required to post or rate.
  • Users rate every idea/comment on at least two axes — Reasonableness (fairly and logically stated; free of obvious fallacy, rhetorical fog, incivility) and Clarity / Lucidity — kept deliberately distinct from "how much do I personally believe it?"
  • The Reasonableness ratings a user receives aggregate into a single account-level Reasonableness score.
  • That account score governs how much weight the user's own votes carry — on ratings and on Graph placements alike.
  • Consequence: hostile, deceptive, or off-point accounts see their influence shrink toward insignificance and their content sink from view; fair, frank, to-the-point accounts see visibility and voting power rise. The right to influence is earned and revocable, so trolling is simply unprofitable.
  • The loop is meant to be self-correcting and stable once tipped toward rewarding accurate, fair assessment.
  • There's a small but real value in separating "is this fairly stated?" from "how much credence do I give it?" so disagreement doesn't masquerade as a fairness judgment.
  • The site is not only an organizer but a competitive arena: each idea carries a comment section where it is sharpened against others, producing new, stronger syntheses. It should let us improve on what we know, not just record it.
  • What "reasonable" rewards: strong grounds for an opinion — well-vetted data and sound math, a well-populated field of compared hypotheses, vigorous open debate, fine-grained attention to components and links, and strong measures against bias — over weak grounds like raw intuition, universal extrapolation from a single experience, or the mere desire that reality be a certain way.

The hard knot: consensus vs. truth

  • Candidly flagged as the central difficulty: a loop that raises credibility for matching others' votes strictly rewards predicting consensus, not truth — and a savvy user could learn to forecast the site's zeitgeist.
  • Three reasons it can still beat anything else available:
    • Schelling point: for a voter blindly trying to match an unknown crowd, the most reliable focal guess is the accurate answer; guessing everyone's biases is a harder, less consistent strategy.
    • It only has to beat the alternatives: the bar isn't perfect truth-detection, only better assessment than is available anywhere else — a far lower, achievable bar.
    • Self-stabilizing: voters bad at tracking consensus lose influence; voters good at it gain it. If consensus can be tied to accurate assessment (via viewpoint diversity), the loop has real stability.
  • The honest philosophical floor: in Reasonable's target domains, truth is not provably determinable — which is why the site must exist. The best achievable is to make perspectives clear and explicit and see where weighted consensus lands; the system claims only the closest approach humanity can currently construct, not Truth.

Sharpening the incentive toward truth

  • Blind initial period: you can't see community scores until you've rated an item yourself (or it passes a vote threshold near its stability point).
  • Author reputation hidden on the face of a post, so a fresh rating isn't biased by past performance.
  • Periodic score updates only, to blunt early-ranking cascades and ease processing load.
  • Randomized blocs: voters are secretly sorted into blocs small enough to have unpredictable, non-representative bias — sometimes mostly one camp, sometimes a clean cross-section, and you never know which. Credibility tracks how close you are to your bloc's average; since you can't predict your bloc's lean, the only stable strategy is your honest best estimate.
  • Lowballer penalty (worked example): shading a 4.2-consensus item to 4.1 lands, on average, farther from the center of the more-numerous higher-leaning blocs than the lower ones — so deliberate distortion costs more credibility than it gains.
  • Self-flagged correction: the extra penalty for deviating from the overall rating beyond your bloc's average is "probably a mistake," since the winning strategy still reduces to guessing site-wide consensus; rewarding closeness to the bloc average is the cleaner mechanism.
  • Proposed early build: a dashboard that simulates different rating schemes and shows the credibility outcomes for users pursuing different strategies — to test the incentives empirically rather than argue them in prose.
  • Weighting collective spending votes: when users direct surplus profit, mere numbers must not sway outcomes — a flood of low-repute accounts shouldn't outweigh a strong majority of high-repute ones.

Honest caveats & the underlying wager

  • Any human-designed system can be gamed by humans determined enough; this will be a permanently evolving area, co-developed with users — and the site's own idea-assessing machinery can be turned on the problem of moderating itself.
  • The whole thing rests on a wager: more people want to build, cooperate, and know what's true than want to manipulate and deceive — the same wager behind Wikipedia, Linux, and the fact that our cities aren't all burnt ruins. If it's false the site fails — but so, ultimately, does civilization.
  • Three load-bearing assumptions stated plainly: (1) enough good faith; (2) ideas are graph-mappable — at least some knowledge gets clearer as a visual graph of stripped-to-core claims, and since all language already strings ideas together, branching strings should work too; (3) strong arguments aren't ultimately fakeable — humans can, with effort and collision and error, reliably tell more-reasonable from less-reasonable, or else our minds are unreliable and we understand nothing.

CLAIMS 04Societal & governmental effects if it succeeds

Science & peer review

  • Make peer review open, detailed, and prospective — not just retrospective judgment of finished papers, but a forum for proposing which information a field most needs and how to design studies to get it.
  • Permanent, visible cross-links between fields, and explicit awareness of which links in a chain of inference are strong, weak, or missing.
  • Result: less weak, un-replicable research, and faster progress against poverty, disease, climate change, AI risk — even death.

Politics & democracy (three readings)

  • As a science of social solutions: finer-grained understanding of the real state of things, and of what proposed policies will likely produce, yields better policy.
  • As theater where the power-hungry must pretend to act reasonably: a public standard of what's genuinely reasonable strips away the ability to dissemble, and lets the public see who is sincere and who is self-serving.
  • As tribal warfare: laying out the legitimate reasoning behind every real viewpoint drains the "righteous" vitriol — while still marking clearly which parts of which beliefs really are malicious or misguided.
  • At the horizon: a far more precise read on the actual public will, less unchecked power for a handful of officials, and possibly a new model of direct democracy.
  • A structural counter to "alternative facts" and fake news: corrupt actors can't easily act on demonstrably weak reasons when strong, clear counterarguments are immediately at hand.
  • The deeper diagnosis: today's best public-discourse tools (blogs, broadcast voices) are "just yelling into the chaos" — what rises is set by who asserts best, not whose ideas are best. The gap between that and a synthesized collective mind is "the difference between acting as one ordered state and a slow-moving, low-level civil war."

Assessing institutions & people

  • All behavior — even corporate behavior — is an implicit claim about what it's acceptable to do, and such claims can be examined and judged reasonable or not. The point isn't to prejudge; it's that these arguments are made implicitly and should be made explicit and publicly critiqued.
  • To dodge the "define ethics" warren, use a pragmatic Approval dimension: an entity's total Approval compounds from all ratings on the set of posts the community agrees are genuinely evaluative of it (and users vote on which posts belong in that set).
  • Examine enough entities and a gradient emerges. Because the examination is granular, every entity gets a precise public map of how to become more trustworthy, and challengers learn exactly where an unloved incumbent is vulnerable — "a tide that, by every channel, flows toward better institutions."
  • The framing claim: there has only ever been one conflict — those who want to control and exploit others vs. those who want to live in reasonable harmony — and the cooperative majority's strongest protection is a clear, shared awareness of the character of the entities with power over them.

Technical innovation

  • Extend prospective peer review into invention: map what could be built and the best paths to build it, engaging every interested mind.
  • Monetizable: an organization specifies a goal and posts a reward for a viable concept-path, split between the site and the users most instrumental in charting it. (The notes also gesture at graph-based programming as a frontier.)

The cultural shift underneath it all

  • People who grow up where this exists are saturated with the awareness that there are plausible arguments on all sides of almost every question — and with how hard it is to out-think humanity's collected best ideas.
  • That mindset — intellectual humility and genuine willingness to have your mind changed — is "the only possible way" to grow individually and collectively; make it common and expected, and arrogance becomes an obvious mistake.
  • A diligent person could come to understand a question better in a short time than career specialists do now, and understand a far wider range of questions.
  • The single most fundamental, far-reaching problem we have is simply that we are not as collectively intelligent as we could be; Reasonable is one concrete attempt to raise that ceiling.
  • Putting money to work: surplus revenue directed, by the site's own collective-intelligence processes, toward whichever interventions the community judges most effective at raising global human flourishing.

CLAIMS 05Monetization approaches it could enable

The premise: invert the adversarial ad model

  • Today's advertising is a zero-sum arms race: advertisers grab attention and harvest data; users deploy blockers and resent the intrusion; incentives aren't merely unaligned but opposed.
  • A cooperative model becomes possible once you can reliably tell which entities people genuinely approve of, and can target offerings to interests without invading privacy.
  • When ads reliably surface things users actually approve of, and users trust that promise, advertising no longer needs manipulation to earn attention.

The mechanisms

Admission is the ad

Only entities in (say) the top quartile of Approval may advertise. Being allowed to advertise becomes the message — "Certified Top 1% / 5% / 10%" tiers carry escalating, sellable value.

Topic-matched, privacy-safe

Because the site maps every field, it can crowd-source which products suit users reading a given topic — relevance without surveillance or personal-data harvest.

An exported ad network

Serve certified, top-approval, tasteful ads to other sites that want to align with their users — Wikipedia being the ideal client: a trusted third party placing neutral, valued ads could fund it without compromising neutrality.

The surplus and what it's for

  • With even a fraction of Wikipedia's traffic, the model could generate net profit well beyond operating costs.
  • That surplus is the point: directed by the site's own collective intelligence toward the interventions judged most effective at raising global human flourishing.
  • Personal profit is capped by design, so that users and advertisers alike trust that neither is being exploited.
  • Innovation bounties (above) are a second revenue stream: paid goal-directed concept-paths split between site and contributors.
  • The Approval-rating data is valuable in itself: well-examined, widely-endorsed organizations will want that judgment publicly known.
