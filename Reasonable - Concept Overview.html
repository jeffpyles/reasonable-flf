A format for collective truth-finding

Reasonable.

A website that crowdsources, organizes, and sorts the world's arguments — the way Wikipedia did for the world's facts — turning the overwhelming flood of human opinion into a navigable, visual map of our strongest reasoning and where exactly it forks.

My synthesis of your background documents · a shared starting point for refinement, not a finished spec · June 2026 · rev. 22 Jun 2026 (see companion Claims Index)

What follows is my understanding of the idea as it lives across your twenty documents — the philosophical fables, the mechanics notes, the rating-scheme arguments, the feature outlines. I've tried to state it back to you as a single coherent system: what it's for, what it would look and feel like, how its incentives are meant to work, and what would happen to the world if it actually caught on. Where I see tension or an unresolved choice, I've flagged it rather than papered over it. Think of this as a mirror to argue with.

PROBLEM 01Why we don't reliably know what's true

The premise begins where Socrates began: knowing what is true, consistent, and reliable is extraordinarily hard, and we are barely better at it than the Athenians were. Most of us cannot defend even our deepest beliefs for more than a few logical steps in either direction — toward their justifications or toward their consequences — and most of us cannot even state the serious alternatives to the positions we hold. If you don't understand the other answers to a question, in what sense do you understand the question?

We have made staggering progress — but unevenly, and for a structural reason. We advance fastest in domains that admit concrete refutation: where reality slaps down errors clearly and repeatably, the way gravity corrects a toddler. That gives us three fields of relatively reliable knowledge:

Technology

The first falsifiable field. That-which-works was so plainly distinct from that-which-doesn't that it barely registered as "knowledge."

Mathematics

The first reliable abstract knowledge — and still the most certain — because error is easy to show, and we delight in showing it.

Empirical science

A disciplined method for checking hypotheses against reality, guarding against our bias toward believing what we wish.

But these three do not exhaust knowledge. The questions humans care about most — ethical, political, religious, social — are often metaphysically intractable, or too complex, or play out over timescales too long to test. They cannot be proven or decisively falsified, yet they govern what we should do with our rapidly growing power. And, as the documents put it, systems whose power exceeds their ability to control that power rarely last. In an era of nuclear weapons, climate change, AI, and cheap bioengineering, our inability to reason reliably in the untestable domains is no longer a philosophical inconvenience — it's an existential liability.

Not having the full chains of justification for every competing claim available for open public comparison is what makes propaganda possible — whoever owns the loudest voice can control the perception of truth merely by drowning out the rest.

When claims to truth are bare assertions rather than references to their entire justifying arguments, the only limits on what can be effectively asserted are loudness, monopoly over the narrative, charisma, and the ability to push the buttons on our irrational minds. "Alternative facts" and "fake news" are symptoms of a missing piece of infrastructure.

THESIS 02The core idea: a 2-D map for a 2-D thing

The deepest claim running through the fables is structural, almost geometric. Speech is bound to time, and time (as we experience it) is one-dimensional — so speech is one-dimensional: it can only pick a single path through the space of everything that could be said, leaving all other paths unsaid and unmarked. When we invented writing, we freed ourselves from time — but out of habit we imported the old linear constraint into a medium that didn't require it. Sentences, paragraphs, pages, books: all single run-on strings, "one and only one of the infinite possible paths, and the roads not traveled almost entirely ignored."

But ideas are not linear. They branch, fork, recombine, and interweave. One claim leads to and from many others. A faithful map of reasoning needs at least two dimensions. Trying to represent that branching web in 1-D strings is, in the documents' own analogy, like being handed a logic puzzle ("the woman in the purple shirt sits two seats left of Bob…") to convey what a single picture would make obvious at a glance. The information is identical; the linear form just strips out the structure of the connections.

So the move is this: take the same impulse that produced language, writing, the printing press, and the internet — each a leap in our ability to share and preserve ideas — and make the next leap. Not more information (we are drowning in it), but a way to map what is known: to draw related ideas together, show how each connects to its grounds and consequences, expose where the edges and gaps are, and reliably assess the strength of every piece. The bottleneck is no longer scarcity of knowledge; it's the chaotic overabundance of it.

One framing from the documents is worth naming directly: every field of ideas has its own possibility space, and the progress of that field is the gradual exploring, mapping, and clarifying of the objects and relationships within it. "To know where you are, where you have been, and how you got from there to here — and what remains unknown — is vital to rational, efficient progress." No medium yet shows the full map of a space: what has been said, which ideas connect to which and why, what is settled, and where the live edges now are. The Graph is meant to be that map.

Wikipedia is for facts

Neutral-point-of-view, consensus overview. Excellent for "where is Timbuktu" — the kind of question a smartphone has already ended pub arguments about.

Optimized for description.

Reasonable is for arguments

The strongest case from every side of a disputed question, stated clearly, placed side by side so they sharpen each other, in context with their assumptions, evidence, and consequences.

Optimized for persuasion & comparison.

The hoped-for cultural echo is precise: just as Wikipedia + smartphones made it socially costly to be confidently wrong about a checkable fact, an accessible public repository of the world's best arguments and counter-arguments would make it costly to expound a weak belief — because anyone who bothered to check would have immediate access to the strongest rebuttal you'd struggle to answer. The aim is to raise the cultural floor for what counts as a reasonable argument, and to tighten the Overton window toward more defensible positions.

ARTIFACT 03The Graph of ideas

The central artifact is the Graph: a single, durable, continuously evolving, user-generated network of ideas, visually connected by lines and spatial position that represent the logical relationships between them. Over time it grows toward a "complete but always-growing ideological taxonomy of the sum total of human knowledge" — not a record merely of what is believed, but of the structured chains of argument and evidence that justify each belief.

An idea is a node: a single claim about the world, reduced as far as possible to its smallest coherent, meaningful component — so it can be examined for clarity, precision, and validity with minimum ambiguity. The ambition stated most plainly in the video script: to bring the clarity and precision of non-quantitative thought as close to the precision of mathematics as is humanly possible.

Argument stacks

A hot question might attract hundreds or thousands of comments. Reasonable lets users collapse them: every distinct version of one single point gets gathered into a stack. Within a stack, the community's assessment floats the clearest, strongest phrasing to the top and sinks the murkiest to the bottom — but nothing is deleted; weaker versions remain beneath, visible, recoverable. The result is that a chaotic question resolves into a manageable handful of stacks, each representing a plausible answer, each showing its best face on top, sitting next to its strongest opposition.

The relational geometry

This is the part I want to make sure I've understood correctly, because it's the heart of the UI. Ideas are connected by four logical relationships, each given a fixed spatial meaning so that position itself carries argument:

Grounds — to the left

The ideas that justify this one; its supporting assumptions and evidence. You read justification by moving leftward.

Dependents — to the right

The ideas that follow from this one; its consequences. You trace implications by moving rightward.

Antitheses — a ring up & down

The ideas that oppose or counter this one — its strongest rebuttals, held in direct visual confrontation.

Alternative statements — stacked in z

The same idea re-expressed: different phrasings, different intuitive framings, different explanatory levels, occupying one point in the plane but a depth-stack behind it.

A node may have any number of each, but it may not hold logically contradictory relationships to the same neighbor (it can't be both a Ground of X and a Consequence of X; it can't be both an Antithesis and an Alternative of X). When users' placement votes conflict, the majority placement becomes the prominent one — but minority placements persist, stay visible, and can grow into the new majority over time. Nothing true-seeming is ever permanently silenced; it's only ranked.

EXPERIENCE 04The map's mechanics & feel

The notes sketch a living, physical-feeling canvas — closer to watching a structure self-assemble than to editing a diagram. Drawing on the mindmap-feature notes:

Two registers of zoom

The macro view describes overall map structure and coherence; it deliberately de-emphasizes individual connections so the large shape reads clearly. The micro view, when you zoom into a node, switches mode entirely — now it lucidly and comprehensively shows that one node's immediate connections. Different jobs, different renderings.

Zoom of explanation, not just of scale

Independently, every concept can be explained at a sliding depth: ELI5 → 10 → 15 → 25 → PhD. The same idea offers an elementary on-ramp and a full frontier-of-understanding account, so a curious amateur and a domain adept can both stand on the same node. (This dovetails with Alternative-statement stacks: explanations pitched to mathematically-inclined vs. literarily-inclined minds.)

Structural affordances

  • Holes as well as nodes — explicit placeholders for things we'd like to know, answers indicated-but-unknown, so the map charts its own frontiers and invites strategies to reach them.
  • Recombining paths — two sibling nodes can share a child; reasoning converges, not just branches.
  • Conjunction — express that node1 + node2 together imply node3, distinct from either alone implying it.
  • Self-arranging physics — the canvas settles toward a lowest-energy layout: gravity-like attraction pulls related structure together while repulsion keeps individual nodes legibly apart.

Reading state at a glance

The proposal encodes a node's epistemic status in its appearance, so the health of an entire region is visible without reading a word:

  • Edge color (red → white): how reliable the rating is — i.e., how much weighted voting it has received.
  • Brightness: Reasonableness. Ideas that sink below threshold fade to deep gray — still visible as "tried and found wanting," never erased.
  • Body color (dark gray → white): Lucidity / clarity.

Bright, white, well-rated nodes = strong, clear, settled. Dim/dark/red = where new and better ideas are needed. The map doubles as a to-do list for editors.

A couple of evocative extras from the notes: a whole-graph "global brain" view where activity ebbs and flows like electrochemical signals between neurons; and a partner history/biography mode that rotates the graph so its long axis runs through time, letting you zoom from past to present with people, organizations, and events positioned over a background world map.

Personal belief structures

A user can map their own belief structure onto the Graph. Then, when a load-bearing node under one of their beliefs is significantly updated or newly challenged, they can be notified — the system pinpoints exactly where, in a disagreement, your chain of reasoning parted ways with someone else's: the last place you agreed, and therefore the last place it's actually useful to argue.

ENGINE 05Rating, reputation & weighted voting

None of the above matters if the map fills with noise. The engine that's meant to keep signal on top is a reputation-weighted assessment loop:

  1. Posting and rating require an account.
  2. Users rate every idea/comment on at least two separate axes — Reasonableness (is this fairly and logically stated, free of obvious fallacy, rhetorical fog, or incivility?) and Clarity / Lucidity — kept deliberately distinct from "how much do I personally believe it?"
  3. The Reasonableness ratings a user receives aggregate into a single account-level Reasonableness score.
  4. That account score then governs how much weight the user's own votes carry — on ratings and on Graph placements alike.

So an account that is consistently hostile, deceptive, or off-point sees its influence shrink toward insignificance and its contributions sink from view; an account that is consistently fair, frank, and to-the-point sees its visibility and its voting power steadily rise. The system is meant to be self-correcting and stable once tipped toward rewarding accurate, fair assessment.

A deliberate separation: "Is this argument reasonable and fairly stated?" is a different question, with its own outlet, from "How much credence do I give it?" — and the documents grant that there's value, probably small but real, in keeping them apart so that disagreement doesn't masquerade as a fairness judgment.

It's worth being concrete about what the Reasonableness axis is meant to track. The Features Outline draws the line between weak grounds for an opinion — raw intuition, universal extrapolation from a single experience, and the mere desire that reality be a certain way — and strong grounds: well-vetted data and sound math, a well-populated field of hypotheses held up for comparison, vigorous and open debate, fine-grained attention to a concept's components and the links between them, and deliberate measures against individual bias. Reasonableness is the score that should reward the latter and starve the former.

The hard part: consensus vs. truth

You're candid in the documents that this is the central difficulty. If a user's credibility rises by matching everyone else's votes, the system strictly rewards predicting consensus, not truth — and a savvy user could in principle just learn to forecast the site's zeitgeist. Three reasons you give for why it can still beat anything else available:

  • The Schelling point. For a voter trying to match an unknown crowd, the most reliable focal guess is the accurate answer. Guessing "what would everyone conclude if they all voted their biases" is a harder, less consistent winning strategy than just assessing the question straight.
  • It only has to beat the alternatives. The bar isn't perfect truth-detection. It's producing better assessments than are available anywhere else in the world — and that's a far lower, achievable bar.
  • Self-stabilizing. Voters bad at tracking consensus lose influence; voters good at it gain influence. If the consensus can be tied to accurate, fair assessment (the role of viewpoint diversity), the loop has real stability and self-correction.

Blind voting & randomized blocs

To sharpen the incentive away from "guess the hivemind," two further mechanisms:

Blind initial period

During early voting, you can't see the community's existing scores until you've rated it yourself (or until it passes a vote threshold near its stability point). Author reputation is also hidden on the face of a post, so prior performance doesn't bias fresh ratings. Scores update only periodically, to blunt early-ranking cascades and ease load.

Randomized blocs

Voters are secretly sorted into blocs small enough to have unpredictable, non-representative bias — sometimes mostly one camp, sometimes a clean cross-section, and you never know which. Your credibility tracks how close you are to your bloc's average. Because you can't predict your bloc's lean, the only stable strategy is your honest best estimate of the accurate rating.

You also worked through how this penalizes a strategic "lowballer": someone shading a 4.2-consensus item to 4.1 lands, on average, farther from the center of the (more numerous) higher-leaning blocs than from the lower ones, so deliberate distortion costs more credibility than it gains. (You later flagged one elaboration of the bloc-penalty as probably a mistake — noted below under open questions.)

A concrete next step you already named: vibe-code a dashboard that simulates different rating schemes and shows the credibility outcomes for users pursuing different strategies — so the incentive structure can be tested empirically rather than argued in prose. This strikes me as one of the highest-leverage things we could build early.

And the honest philosophical floor, stated in your own words: in the domains Reasonable targets, truth is not provably determinable — that's why the site needs to exist. The best achievable is to make our perspectives and arguments as clear and explicit as possible and then see where the weighted consensus lands. The system doesn't claim to find Truth; it claims to find the closest approach humanity can currently construct.

ENGINE 06Not racing to the bottom

The defining failure mode of open internet forums is degeneration into cesspits — anonymity breeding hostility that derails any productive thread. Reasonable's answer isn't a clever filter; it's the reputation loop above, applied as social physics: the right to influence is earned and revocable, and trolling is simply unprofitable because it shrinks your own weight while your content sinks out of sight.

Two caveats the documents hold honestly:

  • Any human-designed system can be gamed by humans determined enough; this will be a permanently evolving area of the site, co-developed with its users — indeed, the site's own idea-assessing machinery can be turned on the problem of moderating itself.
  • The whole thing rests on a wager: that more people want to build, cooperate, and know what's true than want to manipulate and deceive — the same wager that explains why Wikipedia works, why Linux exists, and why our cities aren't all burnt ruins. If that wager is false, the site fails — but so, ultimately, does civilization.

REACH 07Turning the lens on institutions

Once you can aggregate many viewpoints into strong arguments about what's true, you can point that capacity at society's actual actors. The framing: all behavior — even corporate behavior — is an implicit claim about what it's acceptable to do, and such claims can be examined and judged reasonable or not. A soda company implicitly argues that selling pleasurable-but-unhealthy drinks is acceptable; a counter-argument holds that engineering over-consumption makes it predatory. The point isn't to prejudge — it's that these arguments are currently made implicitly and should be made explicit and publicly critiqued.

To dodge the bottomless "define ethics" warren, the notes propose a pragmatic instrument: evaluative posts about an entity carry, alongside the usual measures, an Approval dimension; an entity's total Approval is compounded from all ratings on the set of posts the community agrees are genuinely evaluative of it (and users vote on which posts belong in that set, just as they vote on Graph placement). Examine enough organizations and a gradient emerges — and because the examination is granular, every entity gets a precise public map of exactly how to become more trustworthy, while challengers learn exactly where an unloved incumbent is vulnerable. A tide that, by every channel, flows toward better institutions.

Underneath this sits the strongest version of the claim, from the video script: there has only ever been one conflict — between those who want to control and exploit others and those who want to live in reasonable harmony with them — and in the absence of strong structures, power "naturally tends to fall into the hands of the people who want it most." On this reading the cooperative majority's most fundamental protection is simply a clear, shared awareness of the character of the people and institutions with power over them, so they can reliably coordinate support for the good actors and withdraw it from the bad. Institution-assessment is that awareness, made into infrastructure.

REACH 08A non-adversarial revenue model

The funding idea elegantly reuses the Approval gradient and inverts the usual ad relationship. Today's advertising is a zero-sum arms race — advertisers grab attention and harvest data; users deploy blockers and resent the intrusion; incentives are not just unaligned but opposed. Reasonable proposes the cooperative alternative:

Admission as the ad

Only entities in (say) the top quartile of Approval may advertise. Being allowed to advertise becomes the message — more than the ad's content. "Certified Top 1% / 5% / 10%" tiers carry escalating, sellable value.

Topic-matched, privacy-safe

Because the site maps every field, it can match products to the topics users are reading — relevance without surveillance, no personal-data harvest required.

An exported ad network

Serve certified, top-approval, tasteful ads to other sites that want to align with their users — Wikipedia being the ideal client, since a trusted third party placing neutral, genuinely-valued ads could fund them without compromising neutrality.

When ads reliably surface things users actually approve of, and users trust that promise, advertising no longer needs manipulation to earn attention. And the surplus is the point: with even a fraction of Wikipedia's traffic, the model could generate net profit well beyond operating costs — to be directed, by the site's own collective-intelligence processes, toward whichever interventions the community judges most effective at raising global human flourishing. Personal profit is capped by design.

REACH 09If it worked: the world it points toward

The documents are ambitious about consequences, and I'll relay them at full strength while keeping them clearly in the conditional.

Science & peer review

Open, detailed, prospective peer review — not just retrospective judgment of finished papers, but a forum for proposing which information a field most needs and how best to design studies to get it. Permanent, visible cross-links between fields; explicit awareness of which links in a chain of inference are strong, weak, or missing. The hoped-for result: less weak, un-replicable research, and faster progress against disease, poverty, climate, and AI risk.

Politics & democracy

Three readings of politics, three corresponding effects: as a science of social solutions, finer-grained understanding yields better policy; as a theater where the power-hungry must pretend to act reasonably, a public standard of what's genuinely reasonable strips away the ability to dissemble; as tribal warfare, laying out the legitimate reasoning behind every real viewpoint should drain the righteous vitriol — while still marking clearly which parts of which beliefs really are malicious or misguided. At the horizon: a far more precise read on the actual public will, less unchecked power for a handful of officials, and possibly a new model of direct democracy.

Technical innovation

An extension of prospective peer review into invention: map what could be built and the best paths to build it. Monetizable too — an organization can specify a goal and post a reward for a viable concept-path to reach it, split between the site and the users most instrumental in charting it. (The notes even gesture at graph-based programming as a further frontier.)

The cultural shift underneath all of it

The people who grow up in the world where this exists will be saturated with the knowledge that there are plausible arguments on all sides of almost every question — and with how hard it is to out-think the best ideas humanity has collected.— from the Fable

That mindset — intellectual humility, the genuine willingness to have your mind changed by a strong argument — is, the documents argue, the only engine of individual and collective intellectual growth. Make it common and expected rather than rare, and arrogance and willful blindness become, to the average person, an obvious mistake. The single most important and far-reaching problem we have, in the Fable's framing, is simply that we are not as collectively intelligent as we could be; Reasonable is one concrete attempt to raise that ceiling.

FOUNDATIONS 10What the whole thing rests on

You name three load-bearing assumptions explicitly, and I think stating them sharply is the most useful thing this document can do — they're where the design is most falsifiable.

1 · Enough good faith

More people are willing to be decent and fair than aren't, and they (collectively) outweigh the bad actors. If false, the site becomes partisan muck — but if false in general, we're arguably doomed regardless.

2 · Ideas are graph-mappable

At least some kinds of knowledge become clearer when represented as a visual graph of stripped-to-core claims. How much is unknown until we try and make mistakes — but all language already strings ideas together, so branching strings should work too.

3 · Strong arguments aren't fakeable

Humans can, with effort and collision and error, reliably tell more-reasonable from less-reasonable. If we can't, our minds are unreliable and we can't claim to understand anything at all.

I'd add one I read between the lines as a fourth: that reducing ideas to atomic claims doesn't fatally distort them — that meaning survives decomposition into nodes well enough for the map to remain faithful to the territory. That one feels the most uncertain to me, and the most worth pressure-testing early.

FOUNDATIONS 11Open questions & tensions to chew on

Places where the documents leave a genuine fork, or where building will force a choice. These are my candidates for what we discuss next — not objections, just the live edges.

The consensus-vs-truth problem isn't fully solved — and you know it

  • You explicitly flagged that the "extra penalty for deviating from the overall rating beyond your bloc" idea is probably a mistake, since the best strategy still reduces to guessing site-wide consensus. The bloc mechanism sharpens the Schelling point but doesn't escape the underlying fact that we're rewarding agreement. Is "best-available, viewpoint-diverse consensus" an honest enough proxy for truth, and how do we keep the diversity that makes it one?
  • Proposed early build: the rating-scheme simulation dashboard, to make this empirical.

Reputation systems can ossify into orthodoxy

If influence flows to those who match consensus, how does a correct minority (the Semmelweis case haunts your own documents) ever accumulate the weight to overturn a wrong majority? The "minority placements persist and can grow" rule is the escape hatch — is it strong enough? Should novel-but-unpopular contributions get some structural protection or exploration bonus?

Atomicity vs. meaning

Who decides where one "idea" ends and the next begins? Reduction-to-components is the source of the system's clarity and its biggest distortion risk. How much is decided by users, and how much can/should be assisted (e.g., by an LLM proposing decompositions and placements for human ratification)?

The cold-start problem

Wikipedia bootstrapped on a familiar format (the encyclopedia article). Reasonable asks users to learn a novel spatial grammar and populate an empty graph. What's the minimum lovable slice — one question, fully mapped — that demonstrates the value before the network exists?

Org-assessment & capture

Approval ratings that gate ad revenue create a powerful incentive to game those very ratings — exactly the entities with the most resources to do so. Does the reputation-weighting hold up under motivated, funded adversaries, not just casual trolls?

Role of AI

The original conception predates cheap LLMs. They change the calculus everywhere: drafting alternative-statement phrasings, generating ELI5→PhD ladders, proposing placements, surfacing missing antitheses, even acting as tireless first-pass reasonableness raters. Is AI a co-author of the Graph, a scaffolding tool, or a participant whose votes are weighted like anyone's? This feels like the biggest update the concept needs.

Visualization at scale

A self-arranging 2-D+z physical graph is beautiful at ten nodes. At ten million, does the spatial metaphor still navigate, or does it need to become a queryable database that merely renders locally as a graph? The macro/micro split is the start of an answer; it probably isn't the whole one.

Where this leaves us. I've tried to represent the idea faithfully and at full ambition, while marking the seams. My read: the philosophical core — that we lack infrastructure for reasoning in the untestable domains, and that a 2-D map of argument is the missing format — is genuinely strong and, if anything, more timely now than when you first wrote it down. The hardest unsolved knot remains consensus-vs-truth, and the biggest missing ingredient is a clear account of where modern AI fits. Those two feel like the right places to push next, and a small, sharp prototype — one question fully mapped, plus the rating-scheme simulator — feels like the right way to start turning prose into something we can actually poke at.
