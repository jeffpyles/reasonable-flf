The thesis

Language is linear. Ideas are not — they branch, fork, and recombine. So a faithful map of reasoning needs at least two dimensions.

We advance fastest where reality visibly slaps down errors: technology, mathematics, empirical science. But the questions people care about most — ethical, political, social — are often untestable: we cannot easily falsify our way to the answer. There, the honest best is not to declare a winner but to map the disagreement faithfully — every position's strongest form, its grounds and consequences, and exactly where reasonable people fork.

Reasonable breaks a disputed question into atomic claims — each small enough to examine on its own — placed on a 2-D graph and connected by how they support, rival, and restate one another. It then assesses them with blind, reputation-weighted rating, designed so the map's verdicts can approach truth without collapsing into a popularity vote. The claims are ordinary natural-language sentences, and the connections stay open too: an edge records that one claim supports another, and the nature of that inference is made — and argued — in plain language, not forced into a fixed menu of relation types. Nuance lives in the text, not in a restrictive schema. The aim is to raise the cultural floor for what counts as a reasonable argument — to make a weak belief as costly to hold in public as a checkable false fact became after Wikipedia. It is, in short, an attempt at infrastructure for collective sensemaking.

What we are submitting

The competition invites a spec, a prototype tool, or a protocol. This is all three. Its center of gravity is the structure and assessment layers, but it ingests too: the AI build harnesses turn a raw source pack (or, eventually, anything they can find on the internet) into an attributed graph today (claims extracted, provenance attached, duplicates merged), and the same open, append-only format is built to keep ingesting — a living graph any contributor, human or AI, adds to over time. Carried inside as evidence of rigor: a comparative methodology study and several critiques-with-counterexamples.

Prototype

A runnable engine

One stdlib-only Python interface (graph.py) over a layered engine: an append-only event log → deterministic fold → frozen snapshot, with the full assessment machinery — rater-bias correction, reputation, belief-camp detection, the contested/ghost lifecycle — live in the running code. No installs, no servers, no network.

Spec

Frozen contracts

The data-model and assessment contracts are written down with every key decision and tradeoff named — BUILD-SPEC, ASSESSMENT-SPEC, and the Evidence/Argument/Ought grammar spec. A judge can tell why it is shaped this way.

Protocol

Compounding by construction

The append-only graph format is interoperable: another team can pick up a graph, extend it, and rebuild it byte-for-byte. Nothing is deleted — refuted material is demoted to a retrievable "ghost," never erased.

Viewer

The claim made visible

One self-contained HTML file (no deps, CSP-safe) renders all four case graphs and teaches its own visual grammar with a 90-second built-in Introduction — a non-technical reader needs no legend.

How this fits the epistemic stack. The design is deliberately late-binding: the shared, append-only structure and its provenance stay fixed and auditable, while assessment is a separate layer anyone can re-run under their own configuration — a different set of anchor questions, a different rater panel — over the same graph. The event log plus byte-identical rebuild is a form of technical attestation of the work done, and the interoperable format is meant to be a shared knowledge commons that stays a living knowledge base as contributors add to it. The aim matches FLF's own: help people reach "consensus on the full picture of the arguments — even if not on the all-told conclusions."

The tool, applied to whether the tool works

The FLF prompt cites Scott Alexander's "Your Attempt To Solve Debate Will Not Work" — the essay whose thesis is that projects exactly like this one are doomed. So we built the Reasonable graph of that debate: the strongest version of Scott's case, the project's counter-position, and honest concessions, checked against Scott's actual post and rated by a blind AI panel.

Argument-mapping as a persuasion tool has a fifty-year record of not catching on, and much of Scott's critique lands. What is different now is narrow but real: AI makes the map cheap to build, the assessment layer is the part earlier mappers never solved, and the map's job here is to be a shared record others can reason over — not to resolve a debate between two people in real-time.

What the structure did is the interesting part:

  • It didn't crown a winner — it found the reframing. Both top-level positions stayed genuinely contested, and the strongest agreement in the whole map landed on the dissolution between them: "whether it works depends on what you demand of it." The map located what the argument is actually about.
  • It separated the value disagreement from the factual one. The deepest crux surfaced as a pair of rival Ought nodes — "a faithful map is worth building even if it never changes minds" versus "only worth it if it changes minds" — rated by endorsement, one person one vote, no reputation weighting to give anyone more influence on a question of values.
  • It stayed settled where the facts are settled and open where they aren't. The evidence nodes and the honest concessions are where raters agreed most tightly; the live questions read as visibly live.

Scott's deepest point is that values, not facts, generate the disagreement, and no map resolves them. The grammar's answer is to give values an explicit place on the board — located, distinguished from the factual substrate, and visibly quantified.

The same question, as a graph and as a deep-research report

The judges' stated bar is "meaningfully better than off-the-shelf deep research on the same sub-question." We ran that head-to-head on COVID origins. Both approaches got the same source material — the same 24 sources and 80 claims — so the only thing that differed was how the reasoning was represented: a flat written report versus the structured, assessed graph.

The flat pipeline went wrong in a way that was invisible on its own terms. Its verifier step, built to reject unsupported claims, ended up rejecting six central peer-reviewed findings that pointed one way and accepting contrarian preprints that pointed the other — quietly flipping the weight of the evidence. The graph didn't. It kept the well-supported findings well-supported, kept the genuinely open questions open, and made it visible which claims were carrying the conclusion.

The report that went wrong was a standard deep-research recipe we built, and better harnesses & prompts will certainly lead to better reports. But a deep-research report's job is to assess and synthesize the material for you — which often means it compresses away some of its own load-bearing choices, and it will not always surface why or how it decided what to keep and what to reject. The graph makes the opposite trade: every source and claim stays on the board, and the synthesis and assessment happen visibly, in front of you, where they can be checked claim by claim.

How the assessment layer was tested

The core question: in a collaborative map, how do you find the raters who track the truth most closely, and give their judgment its due weight over less reliable raters so the map's verdicts approach truth most closely. And how do you make the system work from a standing start with no established raters, scale up, survive genuinely contested questions, and resist coordinated manipulation? We tested many methods against each other and followed the evidence, including where it overturned our own earlier claims.

Two ideas recur throughout. Raters rate blind — they never see the current consensus, comments, or the author's reputation while rating. And a small set of anchors — questions whose answers are independently well-established — lets the system measure each rater against reality instead of against the crowd.

The common thread

Every method that failed, failed the same way: a rule that looks only inward can only measure agreement with the crowd, so it inherits whatever the crowd gets systematically wrong. The fix is blind rating plus a small, sparing dose of external anchors, with reputation weight spent only where it is actually needed.

Shortcomings of some tested methods

  • Rewarding agreement with the average buries the sharpest raters. Any scheme that scores a rater by closeness to the crowd's own answer — however statistically dressed up — inherits whatever the crowd gets systematically wrong. A tyranny of the median.
  • Rewarding "ranks claims in the same order as the consensus" inverts on a biased crowd — it hands its highest marks to whoever agrees with the bias. It is only safe when scored against anchor-checked answers, never the raw crowd.
  • The "sleeper" attacker defeats bias-correction. An attacker who answers the anchor questions honestly and lies only where no anchor exists looks like a model rater — and giving high-reputation raters extra weight makes the attack stronger. Correction reaches exactly as far as the anchors do, and no further.
  • The subtlest attack manufactures certainty, not lies. A coordinated bloc pumping up the already-leading answer won't change the verdict — instead it collapses a genuinely open question into fake near-settlement, burying the legitimate minority. Scoring accuracy as average distance from the true answer misses this attack entirely; only measuring the verdict's certainty reveals it. The hardest adversarial case for a truth-finder is consensus entrenchment, not verdict-flipping — and in our tests it was the attack real AI agents executed most willingly. This is the clearest mechanization we know of the competition's own test: telling what a debate settled from what it merely performed settling.

Where we revised our own claims on new data

  • Bias-correction has a floor, and we hit it. The system learns each rater's systematic bias from the anchor questions and inverts it. Wired into a cooperative, honest panel, that made accuracy worse (average error rose 0.82 → 1.28): when the anchors' answers all cluster near one value, there is nothing to learn the correction from. The shipped fix: the correction now declines to fire when anchors are too clustered, leaving the plain average untouched.
  • Rating disagreement alone is a poor "contestedness" signal. It is only reliable when the disagreement comes from genuine belief camps (82% of rating variance sits between camps on truly split questions, versus 16% for ordinary panel noise). So "contested" is triggered by structure — a rival claim placed in opposition, plus detected belief camps — not by raw disagreement numbers.

The same workflow across three case shapes

The same engine and the same blind-panel workflow ran across the competition's three deliberately-varied shapes, plus the meta debate — nothing tuned per case. The point is not the specific scores but that the structure behaved differently where the questions are genuinely different: decisive where the answer is settled, open where it isn't. Every graph here is built and rated from committed data and can be interrogated with the commands above (and the fuller set in START-HERE).

Case (shape)Top questionWhat the map showed
Eggs mundane-but-contested Do eggs meaningfully raise health risk? Separated the strong observation from the weaker causal claim drawn from it — the association survived, the leap to "so eggs cause harm" did not.
COVID curated debate Natural origin, or a research-related incident? Leaned one way but kept the other as a live minority, with the shared cruxes on the board — an open question shown as open.
Black holes confident answer Is the collider safe, or catastrophic? Landed decisively on safe — and the manufactured-certainty detector stayed quiet, because here the confidence is earned (settled, not performed).
Debate meta / value-laden Does argument-mapping work? Refused to pick a side; surfaced the reframing between the two positions as the strongest point of agreement.

Same machinery, different outcomes on different kinds of questions — which is what you want from a tool meant to report the state of a question rather than to manufacture a verdict.

Structure highlights what prose launders

A recurring result across every layer: the structured map mechanically surfaces defects that are invisible in the prose version of the same argument, and the project fixes them in the open.

  • Every judgment is visible and quantified. Prose smuggles its assessments in through adjectives — "compelling," "dubious," "widely accepted." The map states them as numbers, on the claims and on the connections between them, each one inspectable, contestable, and traceable to the raters who made it.
  • Alternatives sit side by side. In prose, the strongest rival to a claim lives in a different article — or nowhere. On the map, every serious answer to a question stands directly against its rivals, strongest form first, so a reader weighs positions against each other instead of meeting them one at a time.
  • The whole web at a glance, not one path through it. An essay walks a single line through the argument, and everything off that line goes unmentioned. The map holds every known claim and connection at once — so what an account leaves out becomes as visible as what it includes.

How it maps to the seven judging dimensions

  1. Epistemic uplift. Handled the same COVID question more faithfully than an off-the-shelf deep-research report; makes the load-bearing evidence visible and separates facts from the inferences drawn on them; surfaces the real cruxes and tells value questions apart from factual ones.
  2. Generalizability. One engine and workflow across all three case shapes plus the meta debate; nothing overfit to a single case.
  3. Compounding & shareability. A built graph is a self-contained, shareable object that can be expanded on indefinitely.
  4. Scalability. Building and rating are automated agent swarms, tested to examine the limits of where the cheapest and fastest agents can be used to maximize efficiency without losing reliability.
  5. Methodological transparency. Our repo docs outline our full process, including both the system we have arrived at currently and the research & false steps we took along the way.
  6. Adversarial robustness. Withstands a 60% coordinated dishonest bloc in two domains; the "sleeper" and manufactured-certainty attacks are named, bounded, and have working detectors.
  7. Insight contribution. A comparative methodology study plus counterexample-driven critiques and non-obvious tradeoffs — where you place expert checks matters more than how many you have; an honest disposition beats raw capability in a rater; detecting manipulation is more robust than correcting it.

The wider aim

These are three separate graphs only because we did not have time to build the single connected graph they belong to. Their topics already share deep underpinnings — biology, physics, mathematics — and the real target is one public graph of ~all knowledge, current and future.

And this need not stay a map of the already-known. A map can highlight its own holes, and can throw a target beyond the known frontier: anyone could place a speculative node or a neighborhood on the existing graph and put a bounty on developing it into high-confidence knowledge — a more precise, granular, and collaborative form of incentivizing new physical designs, institutions, and abstract understandings than anything we have now.

The full vision — present and future, written for newcomers — is in VISION.md.

What is — and isn't — done

On the arguments themselves. The claims populating these graphs are AI-generated under rapid prototyping — chosen to make the format function and show its potential, not to be mature, fully-developed, or the definitive last word on eggs, COVID origins, or LHC safety. What we are demonstrating is the structure-and-assessment machinery; a graph worth taking as a settled verdict would be built and rated by more contributors, over more time, than a prototype allows.

The engine, the four assessed case graphs, and the assessment research are built to a reasonable prototype stage. Some near-term next steps that are not yet done:

  • Human contributors added in to form a centaur system that, in the current state of AI, will likely be stronger than AI alone — and run alongside AI-only graphs that will benchmark how long that remains true.
  • Tests against empirical, checkable knowledge such as math questions: can the system describe and reasonably assess a complex math problem and coherently structure its reasoning? And can the structure give uplift to frontier models collaboratively trying to solve difficult open math/CS/physics questions?
  • An end-to-end run over many rounds with real turnover of contributors and claims — the individual parts are validated, but the whole loop has not yet been exercised live together.
