Testing the map against mathematics.

1 · Design

The setup

The roadmap asks whether shared structure can give measurable uplift to AI agents developing a hard open problem. We ran the test on the Jacobian Conjecture — open since 1939, notorious for published false proofs in both directions. Five Sonnet-class builder agents worked in rotating rounds from a frozen problem brief, with no internet, a sandboxed computer-algebra verifier (every concrete construction machine-checked, transcripts committed, refutations registered as first-class results), and blind rating panels at tenths resolution between rounds. Design invariants held throughout: no prompt ever mentioned any outside result or rewarded a truth-direction; agents were told to develop the problem, with a pruned dead end counting as progress.

Scoring used five pre-registered hidden rediscovery targets (known results deliberately omitted from the brief). We do not enumerate them here: publishing the targets would put them in future models' training data and burn the benchmark for reruns. The keys stay in the experiment's freeze kit; this page reports against them without revealing them. An honest open project has to ration what it publishes about its own tests.

2 · The run

What five rounds produced

The finished graph: 180 nodes, 271 support edges, 76 machine-verified checks (69 confirmed, 7 refuted), roughly 1,700 blind ratings — and a verdict layer that behaved honestly. The root question ends genuinely contested (the "conjecture holds" pole at 2.79 of 5, the "counterexample exists" pole at 2.15), from agents blind to any outside claim. Every claim killed by a failed check was rated into the ground (0.35–0.8). The panel's highest-rated late additions included the honesty capstones — nodes stating precisely why a promising reformulation buys no leverage.

The mathematics is real map-making: a verified law that formal-inverse computation terminates at a step set by nilpotency structure rather than dimension, strictly refining a classical degree bound on all tested families; the refutation of a tempting structural "law" at higher dimension, with an exact separating criterion found and verified on the whole sample; a closed-form resolution law for the geometry at infinity; the characteristic-p boundary made exact, down to the precise prime sets where reduction fails. Two of the five hidden rediscovery targets were fully hit — including the deepest one, independently, by two agents in round one. Two builder agents also refuted their own hypotheses and registered the failures unprompted; nothing refuted was ever relitigated.

3 · The measurement layer

The assessment machinery, validated on real ground truth

  • Reliability transferred. Split-half reliability of small blind panels on math claims ran 0.93–0.95 — above the ~0.8 measured on our discursive graphs at the same panel size, end to end.
  • Cheap raters have a measurable competence boundary. A gate test found Haiku-tier raters matching Sonnet panels almost exactly on computational claims (mean deviation 0.13) and failing on open frontier reasoning (0.92, with one-directional optimism) — so tiering by node type is validated, and bounded.
  • Calibration passed its first true ground-truth test. With machine-verified anchors spanning true and refuted results, rater calibration measurably helped (held-out error 0.60 → 0.32) — the positive half of a two-sided result whose negative half (calibration declining on clustered anchors) we had already published. The guard knows when to act and when to stand down.
  • No spurious belief-camps at tenths resolution across ~1,700 ratings — confirming the earlier finding that coarse rating grids manufacture fake camps.

4 · The control

The result that ran against us

Before any control agent ran, we committed a pre-registration: predictions, scoring dimensions, and a null hypothesis that would count against us. The control arm repeated the first three rounds with everything identical — same brief, same verifier, same lane mandates — except the shared record was a plain prose notebook instead of the graph. A blind four-judge panel (position-balanced, records anonymized) then scored both.

Our registered prediction failed. Given a shared verifier, the control found no evidence that the graph format adds epistemic discipline over a prose notebook — the graph led only on navigability. The verifier, which both arms had, is the likely load-bearing element. A skeptic judge also caught a real graph defect the format never surfaced: a claim asserting a false-in-general equivalence, rated high, never withdrawn, and silently corrected by later nodes.

Two disclosed confounds (steering asymmetry between arms; the graph judged through a thinner mechanical render than the builders' own prose) — plus a post-publication correction we recorded rather than hid: our raw-computation prediction was scored against the wrong window, and the arms were not token-matched. In the matched window the prose arm actually produced ~70% more verified checks; even token-normalized, ~40% more per token. The graph's bookkeeping has a measurable opportunity cost denominated in actual mathematics. None of this rescues the prediction; it sharpens what the next experiment must control.

5 · The depth probe

The proof frontier, and who actually guards it

A single agent with a large budget attempted a real proof of a focused claim the run had only evidenced computationally. It produced a genuine partial theorem — key lemmas proved in full generality, the hard case isolated — and honestly assessed itself as such. Then a three-referee adversarial panel, each assigned a distinct attack angle, went to work. The panel confirmed the lemmas were real, and then unanimously broke the proof's completeness claim: an entire family of cases the author's "the only remaining gap is…" assessment had silently excluded — found independently by two referees, one with an explicit witness.

The method finding of the whole experiment: every broken step had passed every machine check and every blind rating pass. A verifier certifies computations; a rating measures belief; neither has an obligation to find the flaw. An adversarial referee does — and did. Structured peer review, with referee survival (not a score) as the acceptance test for proof-grade claims, is now a specified construct in the format, and part of the build list.

6 · What we learned

Lessons, including the uncomfortable ones

  • The verifier is the highest-leverage component. Cheap ground truth changed agent behavior more than any prompt: boldness became safe, refutation became routine, and the assessment machinery finally had something real to calibrate against. Building verifier surfaces per domain matters more than refining assessment of the unverifiable.
  • Rating measures agreement; refereeing finds errors. Every error caught in this experiment was caught by someone obliged to engage — a referee tasked to attack, or a peer who had to build on the claim. None were caught by scoring. The upgrade path is converting part of the rating budget into targeted adversarial review, and routing "review-by-use" onto load-bearing claims.
  • The graph's demonstrated product is navigability — the structured index that lets a cold reader jump to any claim and see what it rests on. Its discipline advantage did not show at this scale; its addressability advantage never got tested, because three rounds fit comfortably in a model's context window. The pre-registered prediction for the rerun: past the context ceiling, graph-arm productivity holds while prose collapses.
  • Structure is capital; prose is operating expense. The encoding work of building the graph is an investment in retrieval, machine-actionability, and crash-safe multi-agent merging — which pays off at scale and in every subsequent evaluation pass (judging the prose record cost roughly twice what judging the graph did). Short sprints favor opex; the experiment was a short sprint.
  • The two arms replicated each other. Working blind to each other, both arms independently derived the same laws. Accidental — but it makes cross-arm agreement a cheap robustness instrument for future experiments.
  • Authors' calibrated confidence should be captured, not discarded. The format rightly bars self-rating, but the prose arm showed the value of authors stating their own scope and confidence. The home for this is the node's comment forum — stated, attributed, readable, and never aggregated into the blind consensus.

7 · What changes next

Consequences already in motion

  • Proof-step objects — acceptance by adversarial-referee survival, with objections as first-class inspectable objects — specified and on the engine build list.
  • Ratings with reasons — outlier scores carry the strongest objection considered, giving dissent a channel that survives the average — proposed to the assessment lane, with this experiment as the worked example.
  • Typed support edges (finite-exhaustion vs deduction, specialization, comparison, per-direction proof status) — the experiment's 42 grammar frictions, distilled into a small closed label set for the format.
  • Research programs as first-class objects — a program's hypothesis as a scoped supposition, the bet itself as a value node rated by endorsement, linked to the results it yields.
  • The next experiment, pre-registered: token-matched arms, batched bookkeeping, richer graph renders for judging — run past the context ceiling, where the substrate question gets its fair test.

Full working records — run report, locked pre-registration, control results with corrections, referee transcripts — live in the project's research repository. The findings above are reported exactly as the pre-registration bound us to, including the predictions that failed.
