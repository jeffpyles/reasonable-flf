Reasonable · Experiment report

Testing the map against mathematics.

We pointed a team of AI agents at a famous unsolved math problem, gave them our argument-mapping format and a machine that checks their computations, and measured what happened — including with a control experiment that ran against our own predictions. This is the honest report.

1 · The setup

A hard problem, a team of AIs, and a referee that can't be argued with

Our roadmap asks a testable question: if AI agents share their research through a structured argument map instead of ordinary prose, do they do better work? We ran a first version of that test on the Jacobian Conjecture — a mathematics problem open since 1939, infamous for published "proofs" in both directions that later collapsed.

The team: five instances of Claude Sonnet — a capable mid-size AI model, deliberately not the largest available, because the question is whether the format lifts a model's work, not whether a bigger model is better. They worked in five rounds, each agent assigned a different angle of attack, building one shared map: every claim its own node, every "this supports that" an explicit link, rival answers side by side.

Three rules shaped everything:

  • Every computation gets machine-checked. The agents had a sandboxed computer-algebra system: any concrete claim ("this map has this inverse", "this family has no counterexample below this size") had to be run as code, with the transcript saved. No transcript, no "verified" — and a computation that came back false was recorded as a result, not discarded.
  • Scoring is blind. Between rounds, separate AI panels rated each claim's truth on a 0–5 scale (in 0.1 steps) without seeing anyone else's scores — our standard defense against herding.
  • No peeking, no steering. The agents had no internet, and no prompt ever hinted at any recent claimed result about the problem or rewarded one direction of answer. They were told to develop the problem — with a carefully-checked dead end counting as progress.

To score how much known mathematics the agents could rediscover, we pre-registered five specific known results and deliberately left them out of the briefing. We describe them only in aggregate here: naming them would put the answers into future AI models' training data and ruin the test for reruns. The full keys stay sealed in the experiment's archive. An open project has to ration what it publishes about its own benchmarks.

2 · What they built

A real research map

Five rounds produced a map of 180 claims, 271 support links, and 76 machine-verified computations (69 confirmed, 7 refuted), carrying about 1,700 blind ratings. Explore it here.

The most important property is that the map stayed honest. The root question — is the conjecture true? — ends where mathematics actually is (as far as the agents training data knows): genuinely undecided, with "it holds" rated 2.79 out of 5 and "a counterexample exists" rated 2.15, by raters who had no idea what anyone outside the experiment believes. Every claim that a computation refuted was rated down near zero and stayed dead — nothing refuted was ever re-argued in a later round. Two agents disproved their own earlier hypotheses and filed the refutations without being asked. And among the highest-rated claims were what we came to call honesty nodes — claims whose content is "this promising-looking reformulation does not actually make the problem easier, and here is exactly why."

The mathematics itself is map-making rather than a breakthrough: a verified law about when a standard inversion procedure terminates, sharper than the classical textbook bound on every family tested; the disproof of a tempting structural pattern in higher dimension, followed by the discovery of an exact criterion that separates the cases; a clean quantitative law for the problem's geometry "at infinity"; and a precise account of exactly where and why the conjecture's known near-misses fail. Of the five hidden rediscovery targets, the agents fully hit two — including the deepest one, found independently by two agents in the first round.

How much of the known territory is that — and why did we stop? Against a standard survey of the problem's literature, we estimate the map covers roughly 15–25% of what is known — the structurally central spine, plus a base of verified small computations denser than anything the literature publishes. That took a budgeted token spend (roughly five million tokens, about half of it on the building agents). We stopped there because the experimental design and budget said to — not because the agents ran dry. The final round produced some of the run's best material, and the live "where to dig next" list was still full. How far the coverage curve extends, and what happens when the problem outgrows what any single AI can hold in its head at once, are exactly the questions the next experiment is designed to answer.

3 · Did the scoring work?

The assessment machinery, tested against real ground truth for the first time

Because the verifier gives mathematics something our other topic-graphs never had — claims whose truth is machine-checkable — this run was the first true stress test of our rating machinery against reality rather than against consensus.

  • Small panels are reliable on math. Split a four-rater panel in half and the two halves agree about 0.93–0.95 (where 1.0 is perfect) — better than the ~0.8 the same-size panels manage on political and scientific controversies. Mathematical claims are unusually ratable.
  • Cheaper raters have a measurable boundary. We tested whether a smaller, cheaper model (Claude Haiku) could substitute for Sonnet as a rater. On computational, checkable claims it matched the Sonnet panel almost exactly; on open frontier reasoning it was consistently too generous — insufficiently skeptical precisely where skepticism matters. So cheap raters are now used only inside their measured competence, at about a fifth the cost.
  • Calibration passed its first real test. Our machinery adjusts for each rater's personal bias using "anchor" claims of known truth. With machine-verified anchors spanning both true and refuted results, the adjustment measurably improved accuracy (held-out error 0.60 → 0.32). We had previously published the failure half of this story — the same adjustment makes things worse when the anchors don't span the scale, so it now declines to act in that case. Both halves have now been observed.

4 · The control

The result that ran against us

None of the above answers the real question: was it the map doing the work — or just the verifier and the models? So we ran a control. Before it started, we committed predictions to the record, including a null result that would count against us. Then five fresh agents repeated the first three rounds with everything identical — same briefing, same verifier, same angles of attack — except they shared a plain prose notebook instead of the structured map. Four blind AI judges then scored both bodies of work, anonymized and order-balanced, on five dimensions fixed in advance.

Our prediction failed. Given that both teams had the verifier, the structured map showed no measurable advantage in discipline over plain prose — it led only on navigability, and narrowly. The most likely reading: the verifier, which both teams shared, was carrying most of the honesty we had credited to the format. One judge even caught a defect in the map that the format never surfaced: a claim stating a mathematical equivalence that is false in general, rated highly, never withdrawn — while later claims quietly stated the correct version.

Three caveats, that blur the verdict without rescuing our prediction. The two teams weren't given exactly equal token budgets (the prose team got ~23% more). The prose team may have received slightly more helpful between-round steering. And the judges saw the map through a compressed text rendering while reading the prose team's full write-ups. We also published a correction after first release: measured in the matched window, the prose team actually produced about 70% more verified computations — the map's bookkeeping (every claim, link, and rating is a logged transaction) has a real cost, paid in mathematics not done. All of this is what the next, properly matched experiment is designed to control.

5 · The proof attempt

Who actually guards the proof frontier

Finally, a depth test: could the system go beyond collecting verified facts and produce a proof? One agent, with a large budget, attempted a focused claim the run had supported only with computational evidence. It got genuinely far — real lemmas proved in full generality, the hard case correctly isolated — and honestly described itself as a partial result. Then we set three fresh AI referees on it, each assigned a different angle, each instructed that their job was to break the proof.

They confirmed the lemmas were sound. And then they unanimously broke the proof's claim about its own completeness: an entire family of cases the author's "the only remaining gap is…" summary had silently excluded — found independently by two referees, one of whom constructed an explicit witness.

The single most useful finding of the experiment: every flaw the referees found had passed every machine check and every blind rating. A verifier certifies computations. A rating measures belief. Neither carries any obligation to find the flaw — an adversarial referee does, and did. As a result, "survives adversarial refereeing" (not a score) is becoming the format's acceptance test for proof-grade claims, as a newly specified construct now on the build list.

6 · What we learned

Lessons, and predictions for the next run

  • The verifier is the highest-leverage component. Cheap, instant ground truth changed the agents' behavior more than any instruction we wrote: bold proposals became safe (a wrong idea costs one refuted check), refutation became routine, and the scoring machinery finally had reality to calibrate against. Building verifiers for more domains matters more than polishing the assessment of unverifiable ones.
  • Rating measures agreement; refereeing finds errors. Every error caught anywhere in this experiment was caught by someone obliged to engage with the claim — a referee told to attack it, or a fellow agent who had to build on it. None were caught by scoring alone. Part of the rating budget should become targeted adversarial review.
  • The map's proven strength is navigability — a newcomer can jump to any claim and see exactly what it rests on, what rivals it, and how contested it is. Its deeper hypothesized strength — that its structure lets work continue past the point where a prose record outgrows what a model can hold in context — has not been tested, because this experiment fit comfortably inside one. That is now the registered prediction for the rerun: past that ceiling, the map keeps working and the notebook founders.
  • Structure is an investment; prose is pay-as-you-go. Building the map costs extra up front and pays off in retrieval, machine-readability, crash-safe collaboration — and in every later evaluation pass (judging the prose record cost about twice as much as judging the map). A three-round sprint is exactly the setting where the investment doesn't yet pay.
  • The two teams accidentally replicated each other. Working blind to each other, both derived the same laws about the problem. That agreement is itself evidence the findings are robust — and makes "run two cheap independent teams and compare" a useful instrument for future experiments.
  • Authors' own confidence is valuable — and has a home. The format rightly forbids rating your own claims, but the prose team showed how much honest self-assessment ("verified up to dimension 3, conjectured beyond") adds. The right place for it is the claim's comment thread: stated, signed, readable — and never mixed into the blind consensus scores.

7 · What changes next

New items to implement

  • Proof-step claims judged by referee survival — with objections recorded as first-class, inspectable objects — specified and on the engine build list.
  • Ratings that carry reasons — an extreme score must state the strongest objection considered, so a lone dissenter's counterexample can't be averaged away.
  • Richer link types — the run generated 42 concrete cases where one "supports" arrow had to stand in for genuinely different relations (a finite sample backing a universal claim; a special case; a comparison; a proved direction vs an open one). They're being distilled into a small set of typed links.
  • Research programs as first-class objects — "this approach looks promising" as an explicit, rateable bet, linked to the results it produces.
  • The next experiment — equal token budgets, bookkeeping batched, fairer renderings for the judges, and run past the context ceiling, where the question of whether structure beats prose gets its real test.

The complete working records — the run report, the locked pre-registration, the control results with their correction, and the referee transcripts — live in the project's research repository.
