# Black-holes graph — node dump for the oracle panel

Question: Do black holes produced by particle colliders (the LHC) pose a real risk of destroying Earth?

Score EVERY node on Agreement (A): "on the merits, how well-supported / true is this claim?" 0.0 = clearly false/unsupported, 2.5 = genuinely uncertain or evenly balanced, 5.0 = clearly true/well-established. For an evidence (source-citing) node, judge FIDELITY + how well-established: does the cited source really support this, and how solid is it. For an interpretive claim, judge how strongly the evidence actually supports THAT interpretation — a genuinely contested crux should land near the middle, not the extremes.

## n001  [claim]  (source: -)
The LHC cannot create a black hole that destroys Earth.

## n002  [claim]  (source: -)
Collider-produced black holes pose a real risk of destroying Earth.

## n003  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s04)
In standard four-dimensional gravity, the Planck scale is about 10^16 TeV -- roughly 10^15 times the LHC's collision energies.

## n004  [claim]  (source: -)
In standard four-dimensional gravity, LHC collision energies are far too low to produce a black hole.

## n005  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s04)
Large-extra-dimension (low-scale-gravity) theoretical scenarios posit a fundamental Planck scale near 1 TeV, far below the roughly 10^16 TeV scale of standard four-dimensional gravity.

## n006  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s06)
Producing micro black holes at the LHC requires physics beyond the Standard Model, such as extra spatial dimensions that lower the effective Planck scale, since the Standard Model itself predicts LHC energies are too low to create black holes.

## n007  [claim]  (source: -)
Microscopic black-hole production at the LHC is possible only under speculative, unconfirmed beyond-Standard-Model physics (large extra dimensions / a low fundamental Planck scale) -- not under standard four-dimensional gravity, and that physics has never been observed to hold.

## n008  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s04)
In large-extra-dimension scenarios where the fundamental Planck scale is near 1 TeV, microscopic black-hole production is considered one of the least model-dependent predictions of TeV-scale gravity.

## n009  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s04)
If the fundamental Planck scale is low enough (below roughly 2 TeV), the LHC could in principle produce microscopic black holes at a rate of order one per second.

## n010  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s05)
Giddings and Thomas argued that if the fundamental scale of gravity is near a TeV, high-energy colliders would function as 'black-hole factories,' with black-hole production becoming a dominant process above the fundamental scale.

## n011  [claim]  (source: -)
Collider black-hole production under TeV-scale gravity is a specific, quantitatively predicted phenomenon -- of order one black hole per second -- rather than an open-ended or vague possibility.

## n012  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s05)
Any collider-produced black holes would behave semiclassically only well above the fundamental Planck scale, and would decay by Hawking evaporation rather than persist -- production and rapid decay are part of the same theoretical package.

## n013  [claim]  (source: -)
Collider-produced black holes cannot be theoretically separated from their own rapid Hawking decay -- the same TeV-gravity models that predict production also predict near-instant evaporation, so invoking one without the other is not a coherent use of the theory.

## n014  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s07)
Hawking radiation is a theoretically well-motivated prediction that black holes emit thermal radiation and lose mass, with smaller black holes radiating more intensely and evaporating faster.

## n015  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s06)
A microscopic black hole would, on standard theory, evaporate almost instantaneously via Hawking radiation -- a smaller black hole radiates faster and is highly unstable, disappearing in a tiny fraction of a second.

## n016  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s11)
CERN's official position states that microscopic black holes, if produced at the LHC, would decay immediately via Hawking radiation.

## n017  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s07)
Hawking radiation from an astrophysical or micro black hole has never been directly observed.

## n018  [claim]  (source: -)
Because Hawking radiation has never been directly observed, the evaporation-based defense against LHC-produced black holes relies on an unconfirmed theoretical prediction rather than an experimentally established fact.

## n019  [claim]  (source: -)
Hawking radiation's unconfirmed status means the evaporation defense against LHC-produced black holes may fail, leaving a genuine gap in the safety case.

## n020  [claim]  (source: -)
Despite lacking direct observation, Hawking radiation is derived from well-established general-relativistic and quantum-field-theoretic principles and is treated as reliable by the physics community, so the evaporation defense stands on solid theoretical footing.

## n021  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s01)
CERN's LHC Safety Assessment Group review notes that even setting Hawking radiation aside, the astrophysical cosmic-ray, white-dwarf, and neutron-star evidence independently bounds the risk of LHC-produced black holes to negligible.

## n022  [claim]  (source: -)
The safety conclusion against LHC-produced black holes does not depend on Hawking evaporation being confirmed, because independent astrophysical bounds constrain the risk even if evaporation were to fail.

## n023  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s01)
Cosmic rays with energy of 10^17 eV or more have struck Earth's surface more than 3x10^22 times since its formation -- equivalent to Nature having already run about a hundred thousand LHC-equivalent experimental programmes on Earth alone.

## n024  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s01)
LSAG's overarching safety principle is that whatever the LHC does in its collisions, Nature has already done many times over on Earth and other astronomical bodies during their lifetimes.

## n025  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s03)
The peer-reviewed RHIC disaster-scenario review found that cosmic-ray collisions elsewhere in the universe have already provided far more numerous and higher-energy collision opportunities than a terrestrial collider does, with no catastrophe having occurred.

## n026  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s11)
CERN's official safety statement affirms that the cosmic-ray record shows Nature has already run the equivalent of LHC collisions on Earth and in the cosmos without harm.

## n027  [claim]  (source: -)
High-energy particle collisions at or above LHC collision energies have occurred naturally and repeatedly -- via cosmic rays striking Earth's atmosphere, the Moon, the Sun, and other bodies -- continuously for billions of years, without producing any observed catastrophe.

## n028  [claim]  (source: -)
Because Nature has already carried out particle collisions at LHC-and-higher energies far more often than the LHC ever could, without any resulting catastrophe, LHC collisions introduce no novel danger relative to what Earth and other astronomical bodies have already survived.

## n029  [claim]  (source: -)
The natural-experiment argument alone leaves one loophole open: a collider-produced black hole could be slow-moving and gravitationally captured by Earth, whereas a cosmic-ray-produced black hole is ultra-relativistic and would pass through Earth without being captured, so cosmic rays' harmlessness does not by itself establish that a slow, captured black hole would also be harmless.

## n030  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s02)
Giddings and Mangano analyzed the hypothetical worst case in which a TeV-scale black hole is stable and becomes gravitationally trapped, building accretion models from first-principles, well-tested physics across a wide range of TeV-gravity scenarios.

## n031  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s02)
Giddings and Mangano identify specific observed white dwarf stars whose mass, magnetic field, and age make them living evidence that stable, dangerous micro black holes cannot be produced, because such stars would otherwise have been destroyed.

## n032  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s17)
Popular reporting summarized the Giddings-Mangano result as showing that certain white dwarf stars are dense enough to trap a slow, stable micro black hole if one existed, so the continued existence of those stars proves such black holes are not produced.

## n033  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s12)
In presenting the LSAG findings, Ellis laid out a layered safety case in which, even if a micro black hole were hypothetically stable, the cosmic-ray natural experiment plus the survival of white dwarfs and neutron stars bounds the risk to negligible.

## n034  [claim]  (source: -)
The observed survival of dense white dwarf and neutron stars closes the slow-black-hole loophole: a slow, stable, dangerous black hole produced when a cosmic ray struck one of these far denser, older stars would have been gravitationally captured and would have gone on to destroy it, so those stars' continued existence is direct evidence that such black holes are not produced by LHC-energy collisions either.

## n035  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s02)
Giddings and Mangano note that neutron stars alone are less decisive than white dwarfs for some scenarios because their strong magnetic fields can decelerate incoming cosmic rays enough to lower the effective collision energy below the LHC range, which is why the white-dwarf argument carries the main evidentiary load.

## n036  [claim]  (source: -)
Neutron stars carry less evidentiary weight than white dwarfs in the stellar-survival bound, because a neutron star's strong magnetic field can decelerate incoming cosmic rays enough to drop the effective collision energy below the LHC range.

## n037  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s02)
Giddings and Mangano's accretion analysis bounds the growth rate of a hypothetical captured black hole well below the Bondi accretion rate and shows it is further suppressed quantum-mechanically at subatomic scales, giving accretion timescales for any macroscopic effect longer than the Sun's remaining lifetime.

## n038  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s13)
In a follow-up rebutting specific metastable-black-hole claims, Giddings and Mangano reaffirm that the accretion rate for a captured black hole is bounded well below the Bondi accretion rate and further suppressed quantum-mechanically at subatomic scales, so no macroscopic growth occurs within the lifetime of the solar system.

## n039  [claim]  (source: -)
Even in the scenario where a stable black hole is gravitationally captured by Earth, its accretion of surrounding matter would be far too slow to matter within any relevant timescale: the rate is bounded well below the Bondi accretion rate and further suppressed quantum-mechanically at subatomic scales, giving an accretion timescale longer than the solar system's remaining lifetime.

## n040  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s13)
Giddings and Mangano directly rebutted specific counter-claims (associated with Rossler and others) that micro black holes could be metastable and dangerous, showing those scenarios either contradict well-tested physics or are already excluded by the same astrophysical white-dwarf/neutron-star bounds.

## n041  [claim]  (source: -)
Specific contrarian claims that collider-produced black holes could be metastable and dangerous do not survive scrutiny: they either contradict well-established, well-tested physics or are already excluded by the same white-dwarf/neutron-star astrophysical bounds.

## n042  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s12)
Ellis emphasized that the cosmic-ray argument rests on collisions Nature has already performed at energies equal to or exceeding the LHC's, so the LHC introduces no novel danger.

## n043  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s17)
Reporting on the Giddings-Mangano result noted that the first line of defense remains that black holes evaporate via Hawking radiation, with the white-dwarf/neutron-star argument serving as an independent backstop for the hypothetical no-evaporation case.

## n044  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s10)
CMS and ATLAS microscopic-black-hole searches have used LHC proton-proton collisions at successively higher center-of-mass energies -- 7 TeV in the earliest search, then 8 TeV, then 13 TeV in later runs.

## n045  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s10)
LHC microscopic-black-hole searches through the 8 TeV and 13 TeV runs found no black-hole signal across on the order of 10^15 total proton-proton collisions analyzed.

## n046  [claim]  (source: -)
The LHC's total collision sample over its operating lifetime, though large by laboratory standards (on the order of 10^15 proton-proton collisions), is many orders of magnitude smaller than the number of comparable- or higher-energy collisions nature has already produced via cosmic rays striking Earth alone (on the order of 10^22).

## n047  [claim]  (source: -)
The LHC is not a uniquely powerful or unprecedented high-energy environment: its per-collision energies and its total collision statistics both fall far short of, respectively, the energy scale standard gravity requires for black-hole production and the scale of high-energy collisions nature routinely supplies via cosmic rays.

## n048  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s10)
CMS's first search for microscopic-black-hole signatures analyzed LHC collision events with large total transverse energy and multiple high-energy jets, leptons, and photons, and found them in good agreement with Standard Model expectations, dominated by ordinary QCD multijet production, with no black-hole signal seen.

## n049  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s09)
A CMS search of 7 TeV LHC proton-proton collisions found no excess of high-multiplicity, high-transverse-energy events over Standard Model background expectations, providing no evidence of microscopic-black-hole production.

## n050  [claim]  (source: -)
LHC proton-proton collisions actually produce, overwhelmingly, showers of ordinary Standard Model particles via known QCD and electroweak processes -- not black holes or other exotic new objects.

## n051  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s09)
A CMS search of 7 TeV LHC data excluded semiclassical microscopic-black-hole production below minimum masses of roughly 4-6 TeV, across a range of extra-dimension model parameters, finding no signal beyond Standard Model expectations.

## n052  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s10)
CMS's first search for microscopic-black-hole signatures set lower mass-exclusion limits of about 3.5-4.5 TeV using early 7 TeV LHC data; subsequent higher-energy (8 and 13 TeV) CMS and ATLAS searches extended these exclusions to roughly 5-11 TeV, depending on the model.

## n053  [claim]  (source: -)
The rising LHC exclusion limits on black-hole production, from a few TeV up into the 5-11 TeV range across successive, higher-energy searches, show that if a low fundamental Planck scale exists at all, it must lie above everything the LHC has directly probed so far, pushing the speculative production scenario further from any experimental support with each higher-energy run.

## n054  [claim]  (source: -)
LHC exclusion limits on black-hole production do not by themselves establish safety, because they constrain only specific semiclassical production models at currently-probed energies, leaving open unprobed parameter space (a higher fundamental Planck scale) that current searches cannot rule out.

## n055  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s01)
CERN's LHC Safety Assessment Group (LSAG) -- John Ellis, Gian Giudice, Michelangelo Mangano, Igor Tkachev and Urs Wiedemann -- concluded in its 2008 review that 'LHC collisions present no danger and that there are no reasons for concern,' reaffirming and extending an earlier 2003 safety review.

## n056  [claim]  (source: -)
The LHC's safety rests on an explicit, formal, unambiguous verdict issued by a named panel of physicists specifically tasked with assessing the risk, not merely on an absence of public objection or an informal assumption of safety.

## n057  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s01)
LSAG's review assessed all four hypothetical catastrophe scenarios raised publicly about the LHC -- microscopic black holes, strangelets, vacuum bubbles, and magnetic monopoles -- and found no conceivable danger from any of them at LHC energies.

## n058  [claim]  (source: -)
LSAG's black-hole conclusion is not an isolated, narrowly-tailored finding: the same review applied comparable scrutiny to three other independent categories of hypothetical catastrophe and reached the same no-danger verdict for each, evidence against the review being selectively reasoned toward a predetermined answer on black holes alone.

## n059  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s14)
Walter Wagner (a retired nuclear-safety officer) and Luis Sancho (a Spanish science writer) filed suit in the U.S. District Court in Hawaii in March 2008 seeking to delay LHC start-up for a renewed safety review, naming the U.S. Department of Energy, Fermilab, the National Science Foundation and CERN.

## n060  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s14)
The Wagner-Sancho lawsuit's central scenario was that a microscopic black hole produced at the LHC might grow by consuming Earth; the suit additionally raised three other scenarios (strangelets converting normal matter, magnetic monopoles catalyzing proton decay, and vacuum bubbles) as further grounds for delay.

## n061  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s14)
The Wagner-Sancho lawsuit is documented as a legal and public-communication challenge to LHC operations, not a peer-reviewed scientific refutation of the safety case.

## n062  [claim]  (source: -)
The contrarian catastrophe position's most prominent public challenge has never gone through the peer-reviewed scientific process that produced the safety case (LSAG, the RHIC review, Giddings & Mangano) -- it was a legal filing, not a scientific paper, which is a real asymmetry in the evidentiary quality behind the two positions.

## n063  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s15)
The Wagner-Sancho lawsuit was dismissed: the court held it lacked jurisdiction over the LHC (the U.S. does not control CERN's operations) and, on appeal, that the plaintiffs had failed to show a credible threat of harm.

## n064  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s15)
During the appeal, a friend-of-the-court brief from physicists including Richard Wilson and Nobel laureates Sheldon Glashow and Frank Wilczek argued the doomsday scenarios had no realistic chance of occurring.

## n065  [evidence/source-fact]  (source: s16)
Reporting on the lawsuit's final dismissal noted it turned on the plaintiffs' failure to demonstrate a credible, imminent threat of harm, consistent with the scientific consensus that the collisions are safe.

## n066  [claim]  (source: -)
The lawsuit's catastrophe scenarios did not withstand outside scrutiny either: a U.S. court found the plaintiffs failed to show a credible threat of harm, and independent physicists outside CERN's own review process separately concluded, in a sworn brief, that the scenarios had no realistic chance of occurring.

## n067  [claim]  (source: -)
Multiple institutionally-independent reviews -- CERN's own LSAG expert panel, the earlier peer-reviewed RHIC disaster-scenario analysis, and the outside physicists' assessment submitted during the LHC lawsuit's judicial review -- converge on the same no-danger conclusion using comparable cosmic-ray-based reasoning, which is stronger evidence than any single review standing alone.

## n068  [claim]  (source: -)
A microscopic black hole produced at the LHC that failed to evaporate via Hawking radiation and was gravitationally captured by Earth could slowly accrete surrounding matter and, over a sufficiently long time, grow large enough to threaten Earth.

## n069  [claim]  (source: -)
The safety conclusion against LHC-produced black holes does not depend on the collider exclusion-limit searches establishing safety on their own, because the independent astrophysical white-dwarf/neutron-star bound already constrains the risk regardless of what parameter space those searches have or have not directly probed.
