# Rating packet — SARS-CoV-2 origins graph (BLIND: no scores shown)

Question: What is the origin of SARS-CoV-2 — natural zoonotic spillover, a research-related incident, or genuinely unresolved?

## Nodes (id [kind] title — full text)

n001 [claim] Natural zoonotic spillover (market origin)
    SARS-CoV-2 most likely entered the human population through a natural zoonotic spillover from an animal host in the live-wildlife trade, with the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market as the probable spillover site.
n002 [claim] Research-related incident (lab origin)
    SARS-CoV-2 most likely entered the human population through a research-related incident, such as a laboratory-associated infection connected to coronavirus research in the Wuhan area, rather than through natural spillover.
n003 [external_anchor] 73 env-positive / 0 of 457 animal samples positive
    SARS-CoV-2 was detected by RT-qPCR in 73 environmental samples from the Huanan Seafood Market, but in none of the 457 animal samples collected across 18 species.
n004 [external_anchor] Market positives cluster at the wildlife-vendor section
    SARS-CoV-2-positive environmental samples inside the Huanan market were spatially clustered in the section of the market where vendors sold live wild and other susceptible mammals.
n005 [external_anchor] SW-corner stall: elevated positivity + wildlife DNA in every positive sample
    In a wildlife stall in the southwest corner of the Huanan market, SARS-CoV-2 positivity was elevated, and wildlife DNA was present in every SARS-CoV-2-positive sample taken from that stall.
n006 [external_anchor] Wildlife-stall objects: more wildlife DNA than human DNA
    Specific objects at a wildlife stall in the Huanan market -- animal carts, a cage, and a hair/feather remover -- tested SARS-CoV-2-positive and contained more mammalian-wildlife DNA than human DNA.
n007 [external_anchor] Live susceptible mammals sold at the market
    Live mammals known to be susceptible to SARS-CoV-2 were being sold at the Huanan market in late 2019.
n008 [external_anchor] Co-location: virus + susceptible-species DNA
    SARS-CoV-2-positive environmental samples at the Huanan market also contained genetic material from susceptible animals, including raccoon dogs, establishing co-location of virus and susceptible-species DNA.
n009 [external_anchor] Sampling began only after market closure
    Huanan market environmental and animal sampling occurred only after the market's closure: 923 environmental samples were collected starting 1 January 2020, and 457 animal samples starting 18 January 2020.
n010 [external_anchor] Authors' own caution: reverse transmission cannot be excluded
    Liu et al. (2023) caution that the presence of susceptible-animal DNA in SARS-CoV-2-positive samples does not prove those animals were infected, and that human-to-animal reverse transmission cannot be excluded, because sampling occurred in January 2020 after human cases had already been reported and the market had already closed.
n011 [external_anchor] Only two lineages, A and B, before Feb 2020
    Before February 2020, documented SARS-CoV-2 genomic diversity among human cases consisted of only two distinct lineages, designated A and B.
n012 [external_anchor] Both lineages A and B detected in market samples
    Both SARS-CoV-2 lineage A and lineage B were detected among Huanan market environmental samples.
n013 [external_anchor] Dec-2019 cases geographically centered on the market
    The earliest known COVID-19 cases from December 2019, including cases with no reported epidemiological link to the market, were geographically centered on the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market.
n014 [external_anchor] ~1/4 of first 174 Dec-2019 cases linked to the market
    Roughly one-quarter of the first 174 documented December 2019 COVID-19 cases were epidemiologically linked to the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market.
n015 [claim] No animal confirmed infected -- direct pathway unproven
    Because no animal sampled at the Huanan market tested positive for SARS-CoV-2, no specific intermediate host species has been directly confirmed there, leaving the precise animal-to-human transmission event unproven by direct evidence.
n016 [claim] Market as the true early epicenter
    The Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market was the true early epicenter of the COVID-19 outbreak -- the site where the initial human infection(s) that seeded the pandemic occurred.
n017 [claim] Market geography + wildlife co-location together indicate a market spillover
    The convergence of the market's early-case geography with SARS-CoV-2's co-location with wildlife DNA at a specific wildlife stall indicates that a zoonotic (animal-to-human) spillover occurred at the Huanan market.
n018 [claim] Ascertainment-bias explanation of market clustering
    The apparent geographic clustering of early COVID-19 cases around the Huanan market is better explained by ascertainment/surveillance bias -- clinicians and investigators preferentially sought out, tested, and recorded cases with a known market link -- rather than by the market actually being the true site of the initial human infection(s).
n019 [claim] Sampling-timing objection: transmission direction not established
    Because market sampling took place only after the market's closure and after human COVID-19 cases were already known, the co-location of SARS-CoV-2 with wildlife DNA there is also consistent with humans having introduced the virus into the market environment, and does not by itself establish an animal-to-human spillover direction.
n020 [external_anchor] 3 of 73 positive samples yielded live virus
    Three of the 73 SARS-CoV-2-positive environmental samples from the Huanan market yielded live, viable virus upon isolation attempts.
n021 [external_anchor] Worobey et al.'s own conclusion: wildlife-trade origin at the market
    Worobey et al. (2022) conclude that the pandemic's emergence occurred via the live wildlife trade in China, with the Huanan market as the origin point, supporting a natural zoonotic-spillover hypothesis over a lab-associated origin.
n022 [external_anchor] Crits-Christoph et al.'s own conclusion: wildlife-market origin
    Crits-Christoph et al. (2024) conclude that multiple independent lines of evidence corroborate a live-wildlife-market origin of the COVID-19 pandemic via the wildlife trade, rather than a lab-related origin.
n023 [external_anchor] Pekar et al.'s own conclusion: market-based zoonotic origin
    Pekar et al. (2022) conclude that the most probable explanation for SARS-CoV-2's emergence in humans is zoonotic jumps from as-yet undetermined intermediate host animals at the Huanan Seafood Market, favoring a market-based zoonotic origin over a lab origin.
n024 [external_anchor] Estimated dates: lineage B ~18 Nov 2019, lineage A weeks later
    Pekar et al. (2022) estimate the first zoonotic transmission (lineage B) occurred around 18 November 2019 (range 23 October to 8 December 2019), with a separate lineage A introduction occurring within weeks of that event.
n025 [external_anchor] Earliest cases statistically localized to the wildlife-vendor section
    Pekar et al. (2022) found that statistical analysis of the earliest human cases located them to one section of the Huanan market where live wild-animal vendors congregated and where SARS-CoV-2-positive environmental samples were concentrated.
n026 [external_anchor] Earliest lineage-A cases geographically close to the market
    The earliest lineage A cases were geographically close to the Huanan Seafood Market.
n027 [external_anchor] Lineage A/B defined by two SNVs
    SARS-CoV-2 lineages A and B are distinguished by two single-nucleotide variants, at genome positions 8782 and 28144.
n028 [external_anchor] Two-spillover model logically requires an unsampled A-B intermediate
    Because lineages A and B differ by only two SNVs, the two-spillover hypothesis logically requires that a single-SNV A-B intermediate genome existed in an unsampled animal host; finding such an intermediate genome among human cases would be inconsistent with a strict two-spillover model.
n029 [external_anchor] Pekar et al.: two separate zoonotic transmissions
    Pekar et al. (2022) report that phylodynamic rooting methods combined with epidemic simulations indicate SARS-CoV-2 lineages A and B resulted from at least two separate zoonotic transmission events into humans, rather than one spillover.
n030 [external_anchor] Pekar et al.: Bayes factor ~60 for two introductions
    Pekar et al. (2022) reported Bayes factors of approximately 60 favoring two separate zoonotic introductions of SARS-CoV-2 over a single-introduction model.
n031 [external_anchor] Pekar et al. excluded 20 intermediate-haplotype genomes
    Pekar et al. (2022) excluded 20 SARS-CoV-2 genomes with intermediate lineage A/B haplotypes from their two-spillover phylogenetic analysis, citing low sequencing read depth and likely erroneous base calls at the two lineage-defining positions.
n032 [external_anchor] GISAID depth data contradict exclusion rationale
    GISAID sequencing-depth data show that most of the 20 excluded intermediate-haplotype genomes were in fact sequenced to high average read depth, contradicting the low-depth rationale Pekar et al. gave for excluding them.
n033 [external_anchor] Critique: polytomy-biased model produces artifact
    A bioRxiv critique argues the phylodynamic model Pekar et al. used is biased against basal (lineage A/B) polytomies, so their finding of a low probability of a basal polytomy under a single-introduction scenario is a model artifact rather than genuine evidence for two spillovers.
n034 [external_anchor] McCowan: Bayes factor is an artefact of imbalanced framework
    McCowan (2025) argues the ~60 Bayes-factor support Pekar et al. reported for two introductions is an artefact of an imbalanced hypothesis-testing framework, in which the single-introduction model was held to more stringent acceptance conditions than the two-introduction model.
n035 [external_anchor] McCowan: support vanishes under matched conditions
    McCowan (2025) reports that when the two-introduction model is subjected to the same acceptance conditions imposed on the single-introduction model, the quantitative support for multiple introductions disappears.
n036 [external_anchor] McCowan: three specific asymmetric conditions identified
    McCowan (2025) identifies the specific asymmetric conditions driving the inflated two-introduction Bayes factor as a relative-clade-size condition, an evolutionary-separation condition, and a root-shape condition, each applied unequally to the two competing models.
n037 [external_anchor] Bloom: outgroup- vs date-based rooting conflict
    Bloom (2025) reports that the two standard phylogenetic rooting principles conflict for SARS-CoV-2: outgroup-based rooting (favoring lineage A as ancestral) and date-based rooting (favoring the earlier, market-linked lineage B) point to different roots.
n038 [external_anchor] Bloom: tree root cannot be confidently placed
    Bloom (2025) concludes the root of the SARS-CoV-2 phylogenetic tree cannot be confidently placed between lineage A and lineage B with current genomic data, meaning genomic/phylogenetic data alone cannot definitively establish whether the pandemic began with one or two independent introductions.
n039 [external_anchor] Bloom: sequence bias as a candidate explanation for the rooting conflict
    Bloom (2025) proposes, as one possible (not confirmed) explanation, that the discrepancy between outgroup- and date-based rooting may arise from biases among the earliest available SARS-CoV-2 sequences.
n040 [claim] Two independent spillovers (lineage A + B)
    SARS-CoV-2 lineages A and B most likely arose from two separate zoonotic spillover events at the Huanan market rather than a single introduction.
n041 [claim] Single introduction, diversified early
    SARS-CoV-2 lineages A and B are more plausibly explained by a single zoonotic introduction followed by early within-human diversification, given that the phylogenetic root cannot be confidently placed between them and that the genomic case for two separate introductions rests on contested exclusion criteria and model assumptions.
n042 [claim] Introduction count is genomically indeterminate
    Whether the SARS-CoV-2 pandemic began from one or two independent zoonotic introductions cannot currently be determined from genomic/phylogenetic evidence alone.
n043 [external_anchor] Intact FCS retained in early isolates
    Early SARS-CoV-2 clinical isolates retained an intact furin cleavage site in the spike protein, whereas that site tends to be deleted during laboratory cell-culture handling.
n044 [external_anchor] ENaC similarity is coincidental
    The 8-amino-acid similarity (RRARSVAS) between the SARS-CoV-2 spike furin cleavage site and a furin cleavage site in the human epithelial sodium channel (ENaC) is coincidental rather than the product of deliberate engineering.
n045 [external_anchor] Codon usage argues against ENaC-copying
    Except for a single codon (encoding arginine 685), every codon encoding the RRARSVAS motif differs between the human ENaC gene and SARS-CoV-2 -- a pattern not expected if the SARS-CoV-2 sequence had been copied from the human ENaC gene.
n046 [external_anchor] RSVAS motif occurs naturally in BANAL-20-52
    Five of the eight amino acids (RSVAS) in or near the ENaC-like motif are already present in the spike proteins of naturally occurring sarbecoviruses such as BANAL-20-52, showing the motif can arise naturally in related coronaviruses without a human-ENaC template.
n047 [external_anchor] Harrison & Sachs' ENaC-mimicry engineering accusation
    Harrison and Sachs have argued that UNC and Wuhan Institute of Virology coronavirologists engineered the SARS-CoV-2 furin cleavage site to mimic the human ENaC furin cleavage site, in order to make the virus more infectious for human lung epithelia.
n048 [external_anchor] Rebuttal: a designer would not have chosen ENaC's site
    A PNAS rebuttal argues that a researcher deliberately designing a furin cleavage site to enhance infectivity would logically have copied a known coronavirus furin cleavage site rather than the human ENaC site, undercutting the ENaC-mimicry engineering scenario.
n049 [external_anchor] Proximal Origin paper's published conclusion
    "The Proximal Origin of SARS-CoV-2" (Andersen, Rambaut, Lipkin, Holmes, Garry; Nature Medicine, 17 March 2020) concluded the virus arose by natural zoonotic spillover and explicitly rejected a laboratory origin.
n050 [external_anchor] Authors privately weighed a lab origin during drafting
    While drafting the Proximal Origin paper, the authors privately considered a laboratory origin, including the possibility that the furin cleavage site arose via serial passage, and acknowledged the evidence did not rule out an accidental laboratory infection -- in tension with the confident public conclusion they ultimately published.
n051 [external_anchor] Early draft's serial-passage discussion was altered
    An early draft of the Proximal Origin paper stated that furin cleavage sites can arise in betacoronaviruses in the laboratory through serial passage, but this discussion was altered before the final published version dismissed the laboratory scenario.
n052 [external_anchor] Published case rested on RBD + FCS features
    The Proximal Origin paper's published case for natural origin rested on specific genomic features -- the receptor-binding domain and the spike-protein furin cleavage site -- which the authors argued were more consistent with natural selection than with laboratory engineering.
n053 [external_anchor] No single genomic feature is individually dispositive
    A critical analysis of the SARS-CoV-2 origin evidence concludes that no single viral genome feature, including the furin cleavage site or the receptor-binding domain's ACE2-binding residues, provides definitive evidence for either the natural or the laboratory origin hypothesis, since each feature is consistent with both natural evolution and laboratory scenarios such as serial passage.
n054 [claim] FCS arose naturally
    The SARS-CoV-2 furin cleavage site most likely arose through a natural evolutionary process, and its partial similarity to the human ENaC cleavage motif is best explained as coincidence rather than deliberate engineering.
n055 [claim] FCS was deliberately engineered
    The SARS-CoV-2 furin cleavage site was most likely inserted through deliberate genetic engineering, potentially designed to mimic a human ENaC-like motif to enhance infectivity for human lung epithelia.
n056 [claim] FCS arose via unintentional lab passage
    The SARS-CoV-2 furin cleavage site could plausibly have arisen unintentionally through serial passage of a progenitor virus in laboratory cell culture, rather than through deliberate design or a purely natural evolutionary pathway.
n057 [claim] Intact FCS weighs against a passage-history origin
    The persistence of an intact furin cleavage site in early SARS-CoV-2 clinical isolates is more consistent with an origin that did not involve extended laboratory cell-culture passage than with one that did.
n058 [claim] Genomic evidence alone is inconclusive on origin type
    Taken on its own, the genomic and phylogenetic evidence for SARS-CoV-2 does not decisively favor either a purely natural zoonotic origin or a research-related incident: the furin cleavage site's origin, receptor-binding adaptation, and the lineage-introduction count are each open questions rather than settled discriminators.
n059 [claim] On balance, genomic evidence favors natural origin
    Although no single genomic feature is individually dispositive, the balance of genomic and phylogenetic evidence -- the furin cleavage site's natural explanations, the rebuttal of ENaC-mimicry engineering, and the market-linked lineage evidence -- favors a natural zoonotic origin over a research-related incident.
n060 [external_anchor] Critique: unaccounted-for sequencing/ascertainment bias
    A bioRxiv critique concludes that the phylogenetic/phylodynamic evidence does not robustly distinguish two independent zoonotic introductions from a single introduction, because the analyses fail to account for ascertainment and sampling biases in how early Wuhan cases were detected and sequenced.
n061 [external_anchor] Critique authors: exclusion unfounded, two-spillover conclusion unsupported
    The authors of the intermediate-genome-exclusion critique conclude that Pekar et al.'s exclusion of the 20 A-B intermediate genomes is unfounded, which leaves the conclusion of two independent natural zoonoses unsupported.
n062 [external_anchor] mBio critical analysis: two lineages consistent with multiple spillovers
    A critical-analysis paper (mBio) interprets the two genetically distinct early lineages (A and B) as consistent with multiple independent spillover events into humans.
n063 [external_anchor] Candidate intermediate-host species detected at the market
    The wildlife species whose DNA co-occurred with SARS-CoV-2-positive samples at the Huanan market include civets, bamboo rats, and raccoon dogs -- all previously proposed as candidate intermediate hosts for SARS-CoV-2 -- with raccoon dogs and hoary bamboo rats the most abundant mammalian wildlife detected.
n064 [external_anchor] Bloom reanalysis: raccoon-dog DNA and viral RNA don't reliably co-occur
    In the Huanan market environmental samples, SARS-CoV-2 sequencing reads are not consistently correlated with reads from susceptible non-human animals such as raccoon dogs: most samples with abundant raccoon-dog genetic material contained little or no SARS-CoV-2.
n065 [external_anchor] Market-data dispute is interpretive, not factual
    Bloom's reanalysis and the original China CDC / Crits-Christoph et al. analysis of the Huanan market metagenomic data agree on the underlying dataset -- that samples contain genetic material from many species, including susceptible raccoon dogs -- and disagree only over interpretation, not over which genetic material was present.
n066 [external_anchor] SAGO: zoonotic origin most likely
    The WHO Scientific Advisory Group for the Origins of Novel Pathogens (SAGO) concluded that the weight of scientific evidence supports SARS-CoV-2 first entering humans either directly from bats or via an intermediate host, making a zoonotic (animal-source) origin the most likely explanation.
n067 [external_anchor] SAGO: inconclusive absent more data, cannot rule out lab origin
    Despite favoring an animal origin, SAGO stated the origins of SARS-CoV-2 will remain inconclusive until more data becomes available, and it could not rule out a laboratory-related origin, largely because of data gaps -- including unshared upstream information on the farms and traders supplying animals to Wuhan markets -- attributed to China.
n068 [external_anchor] Intermediate host presumed but unconfirmed
    An intermediate host is presumed under the zoonotic-transmission model for SARS-CoV-2 (bats proposed as the natural reservoir, pangolins previously proposed as a candidate intermediate host), but the identity -- and even the existence -- of a confirmed intermediate host remains controversial and unconfirmed.
n069 [external_anchor] Host niche inadequately sampled
    The specific intermediate host and viral lineage that seeded the pandemic remain unidentified because the wildlife host niche harboring the direct precursors of SARS-CoV-2 has not been adequately sampled.
n070 [claim] Historical base rate favors zoonotic spillover
    Most previously identified coronaviruses that emerged in the human population, including SARS-CoV-1 (linked to palm civets and other small mammals sold in live-animal markets in southern China) and MERS-CoV (linked to dromedary camels), originated through zoonotic spillover from an animal reservoir, often via an intermediate host associated with live-animal trade; this historical base rate favors zoonotic spillover as the default explanation for a novel human coronavirus, absent case-specific evidence to the contrary.
n071 [claim] Base rate weakened by case-specific lab proximity
    The historical base rate from prior coronavirus spillovers carries reduced evidential weight for SARS-CoV-2 specifically, because this outbreak is the first to emerge in a city that also hosted a major laboratory conducting active research on closely related bat coronaviruses -- a case-specific circumstance absent from the SARS-1 and MERS precedents the base rate is drawn from.
n072 [claim] SARS-1 base-rate precedent: civets/markets
    SARS-CoV-1, which caused the 2002-2003 SARS outbreak, is understood to have entered the human population via zoonotic spillover from an animal reservoir, with palm civets and other small mammals sold in live-animal markets in southern China implicated in the intermediate-host pathway.
n073 [claim] MERS base-rate precedent: camels
    MERS-CoV is understood to have entered the human population via zoonotic spillover, with dromedary camels serving as the primary animal reservoir and intermediate-host pathway.
n074 [claim] Base rate favors zoonotic spillover (generalization)
    Prior emerging human coronaviruses have predominantly originated through zoonotic spillover from an animal reservoir via an intermediate host, often associated with live-animal trade, establishing a historical base rate that favors zoonotic spillover as the default explanation for a novel human coronavirus, absent case-specific evidence to the contrary.
n075 [claim] Host not found YET -- consistent with prior-outbreak timelines
    The absence to date of an identified infected intermediate host is unsurprising and not strong evidence against a zoonotic origin, because comparable animal-source investigations for SARS-1 and MERS also took months to years after those outbreaks were recognized, and in some respects were never fully resolved.
n076 [claim] Host not found DESPITE search -- evidentially telling
    The failure to identify any infected intermediate host despite substantial, targeted sampling of animals at and around the Huanan market is evidentially significant and weakens confidence in a market-based zoonotic-spillover pathway specifically.
n077 [external_anchor] Bloom: co-mingling data uninformative once virus already widespread
    The co-mingling of viral and animal genetic material in the January 2020 market environmental samples is unlikely to reveal which species, if any, was an intermediate host, because SARS-CoV-2 was already widespread throughout the market by the time those samples were collected.
n078 [external_anchor] WIV located in Wuhan
    The Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV) is a coronavirus research laboratory located in Wuhan, China -- the same city in which the COVID-19 outbreak was first identified, and the subject of a dedicated 2023 U.S. intelligence assessment of potential links to the pandemic's origin.
n079 [external_anchor] IC: probably not genetically engineered
    Most U.S. intelligence-community agencies assessed, with low confidence, that SARS-CoV-2 was probably not genetically engineered, while two agencies found insufficient evidence to assess the question either way.
n080 [external_anchor] WIV had coronavirus-engineering capability
    The declassified June 2023 ODNI report confirms that the Wuhan Institute of Virology had the technical capability to create and genetically engineer novel coronaviruses, including chimeric viruses, before the pandemic.
n081 [external_anchor] Fall-2019 WIV staff illnesses (inconclusive)
    Several Wuhan Institute of Virology staff were ill with respiratory or other symptoms in the fall of 2019, but the U.S. intelligence community judged these illnesses did not clearly point to COVID-19.
n082 [external_anchor] No IC evidence WIV possessed a progenitor
    The declassified U.S. intelligence assessment did not identify direct evidence that the Wuhan Institute of Virology possessed SARS-CoV-2 or a close progenitor virus before the pandemic began.
n083 [external_anchor] IC split: 5+NIC natural vs FBI+DOE lab
    The ODNI report on potential WIV links to COVID-19's origin, mandated by the COVID-19 Origin Act of 2023 and declassified on 23 June 2023, documents that the U.S. intelligence community remained divided: five agencies plus the National Intelligence Council favored a natural-spillover origin, while the FBI and Department of Energy favored a lab-associated origin.
n084 [external_anchor] 2016 NIH gain-of-function pause inquiry
    On 31 March 2016, NIH notified the University of Minnesota that a coronavirus research grant involving Ralph Baric might fall under the federal gain-of-function funding pause, and asked the researchers to clarify whether the work could produce pathogens with enhanced traits in mammals via the respiratory route.
n085 [external_anchor] 'Experiment 4': recombinant SARS-like virus creation
    A component of that grant, labeled 'Experiment 4,' proposed creating live recombinant SARS-like coronaviruses with engineered mutations to test how efficiently the resulting viruses could infect different species.
n086 [external_anchor] DEFUSE proposal (2018, unfunded)
    The 2018 DEFUSE grant proposal, coauthored by Wuhan Institute of Virology researcher Zhengli Shi, EcoHealth Alliance, and UNC's Ralph Baric, proposed engineering high-risk SARS-related coronaviruses -- including cleavage-site insertions and spike-protein swaps -- but was never funded.
n087 [external_anchor] Baric-Shi coronavirus research collaboration
    Ralph Baric, a UNC virologist who pioneered coronavirus reverse-genetics ('no-see-um'/seamless-cloning) systems, had a years-long research collaboration with Wuhan Institute of Virology researcher Zhengli Shi studying how coronaviruses adapt to new hosts.
n088 [external_anchor] IC: both origins remain plausible, none certain
    As of the June 2023 declassified report, every U.S. intelligence-community agency continued to assess that both a natural and a laboratory-associated origin remained plausible hypotheses for the first human SARS-CoV-2 infection; none reached certainty.
n089 [external_anchor] CIA + 1 agency: unable to determine
    The CIA and one other unidentified U.S. intelligence agency remained unable to determine the precise origin of SARS-CoV-2, citing that both hypotheses rely on significant assumptions or face conflicting reporting.
n090 [external_anchor] COVID-19 Origin Act (2023): unanimous, signed
    The June 2023 declassified U.S. intelligence report was released as required by the COVID-19 Origin Act of 2023, which passed both chambers of Congress unanimously and was signed into law by President Biden.
n091 [external_anchor] DOE (low conf.) and FBI (moderate conf.) lab assessments
    As of its March 2023 publication, reporting indicated the U.S. Department of Energy had assessed with 'low confidence' that a laboratory-associated incident was the most likely origin of the first human SARS-CoV-2 infection, while the FBI assessed a lab origin with 'moderate confidence.'
n092 [external_anchor] FBI/DOE reasoning: circumstantial, undisclosed
    Reporting indicates the FBI's and DOE's lab-origin assessments are not based on scientific analysis of the virus itself but on circumstantial arguments, and the specific reasoning and evidence behind them was not publicly disclosed.
n093 [claim] Lab capability + location is significant
    The co-location of a laboratory with documented capability to create and genetically engineer novel coronaviruses in the same city where the COVID-19 pandemic first emerged is evidentially significant, raising the plausibility of a research-related origin above what mere chance geographic proximity would suggest.
n094 [claim] Lab-city co-location is weak, confounded evidence
    Because major biomedical research centers are concentrated in large cities worldwide, and Wuhan is itself a large hub independently connected to the wildlife trade and to a market later identified as the epidemic's early epicenter, the co-location of a capable coronavirus lab and the outbreak provides only weak, easily-confounded evidential weight for a research-related origin on its own.
n095 [claim] Absence of found progenitor doesn't rule out lab origin
    That a dedicated U.S. intelligence investigation did not surface evidence of SARS-CoV-2 or a close progenitor virus at the Wuhan Institute of Virology does not meaningfully weigh against a research-related origin, because an accidental exposure to an already-collected but unpublished or unsequenced field sample would be expected to leave no documentary trail for outside investigators to find.
n096 [claim] No found progenitor is a real gap for lab hypothesis
    The absence of any evidence -- after a dedicated U.S. intelligence investigation -- that the Wuhan Institute of Virology possessed SARS-CoV-2 or a close progenitor virus before the pandemic is a significant evidential gap in the research-related-incident hypothesis, since its central mechanism (an already-present virus escaping the lab) remains empirically unconfirmed.
n097 [claim] Sick WIV staff supports lab-infection scenario
    That multiple Wuhan Institute of Virology staff fell ill with respiratory or other symptoms in the fall of 2019 -- around the period later estimated for the virus's emergence -- is consistent with, and lends some support to, a laboratory-associated infection event.
n098 [claim] Sick WIV staff report carries little specific weight
    Because the U.S. intelligence community itself judged that the fall-2019 Wuhan Institute of Virology staff illnesses did not clearly point to COVID-19, and respiratory illness among laboratory staff during ordinary cold-and-flu season is unremarkable on its own, the reported illness cluster carries little specific evidential weight for a laboratory-associated origin.
n099 [claim] DEFUSE shows engineering intent pre-existed
    That researchers connected to the Wuhan Institute of Virology proposed in 2018, under the DEFUSE grant, to engineer high-risk SARS-related coronaviruses using techniques including cleavage-site insertions and spike-protein swaps demonstrates that the specific genetic modifications hypothesized under an engineered-origin scenario were actively conceived of and sought funding by researchers connected to the lab shortly before the pandemic.
n100 [claim] DEFUSE was unfunded intent, not demonstrated action
    Because the DEFUSE proposal was never funded and no documented evidence shows any of its proposed experiments were actually carried out, the proposal establishes that researchers sought funding for a specific research design, not that the described engineering techniques were ever applied to a virus that became SARS-CoV-2.
n101 [claim] FBI/DOE lab assessments lend some support
    That the FBI and the Department of Energy -- agencies with access to classified intelligence not available to outside scientists -- independently assessed a laboratory-associated incident as the more likely origin of SARS-CoV-2 lends some support to the research-related-incident hypothesis, notwithstanding their low-to-moderate confidence levels.
n102 [claim] IC split is weak, contested support for lab origin
    Because the FBI's and Department of Energy's lab-origin assessments were reached at low-to-moderate confidence, rest on reportedly circumstantial and undisclosed reasoning rather than published scientific analysis, and are outnumbered by the National Intelligence Council and four other agencies favoring natural spillover, the U.S. intelligence community's split verdict provides only weak and contested support for a laboratory-associated origin.
n103 [claim] Lab-context case: credible, non-negligible hypothesis
    Taken together, the Wuhan Institute of Virology's documented capability to engineer novel coronaviruses, the DEFUSE proposal's demonstrated intent to create similar genetic modifications, the reported fall-2019 staff illnesses, and the divided U.S. intelligence assessments make a research-related incident a credible, non-negligible hypothesis for SARS-CoV-2's origin.
n104 [claim] Lab-context case remains circumstantial, unconfirmed
    Because no direct evidence connects the Wuhan Institute of Virology to a specific SARS-CoV-2 progenitor virus or to an actual transmission event, and because the intelligence community's own lab-origin assessments are low-confidence, contested, and explicitly not grounded in disclosed scientific analysis, the case for a research-related incident remains circumstantial relative to the documented, sample-based case for market-based zoonotic spillover.
n105 [external_anchor] JVI critical analysis: unresolved, and for lack of data
    A critical analysis of the SARS-CoV-2 origin evidence (Journal of Virology, 2023) concludes that the two leading origin hypotheses cannot currently be distinguished or falsified with the available evidence, so a definitive determination of SARS-CoV-2's origin is unlikely, and attributes this primarily to missing data rather than to existing evidence being conclusive for either side; it holds the lab-leak hypothesis in particular cannot be confirmed without new evidence.
n106 [external_anchor] JVI: two hypotheses currently indistinguishable
    A critical analysis of the SARS-CoV-2 origin evidence (Journal of Virology, 2023) concludes that the two leading origin hypotheses cannot currently be distinguished or falsified with the available evidence, so a definitive determination of SARS-CoV-2's origin is unlikely.
n107 [external_anchor] JVI: unresolved because of missing data, not conclusive evidence
    The critical analysis attributes the origin debate's unresolved status primarily to missing data rather than to existing evidence being conclusive for either hypothesis, and holds that the lab-leak hypothesis in particular cannot be confirmed without new evidence.
n108 [claim] Origins question remains genuinely unresolved overall
    Given the totality of currently available evidence -- genomic, epidemiological, and intelligence-derived alike, not genomic evidence alone -- whether SARS-CoV-2 arose via natural zoonotic spillover or a research-related incident remains genuinely unresolved and cannot currently be definitively determined.
n109 [claim] IC majority's natural-origin lean supports zoonotic hypothesis
    That the National Intelligence Council and a majority of U.S. intelligence-community agencies independently assessed natural animal exposure as the more likely origin of SARS-CoV-2 lends some support to the natural zoonotic-spillover hypothesis, notwithstanding their low confidence levels.
n110 [evidence] Endonuclease 'fingerprint' (Bruttel 2022)
    Bruttel, Washburne & VanDongen (2022, bioRxiv) reported that SARS-CoV-2's genome carries a pattern of BsaI/BsmBI restriction sites -- regular spacing and silent mutations concentrated at those sites -- that they argue matches an idealized in-vitro reverse-genetics assembly system and is anomalous relative to natural coronaviruses.
n111 [claim] Restriction pattern = synthetic-assembly fingerprint
    The BsaI/BsmBI restriction-site pattern in the SARS-CoV-2 genome fits an idealized reverse-genetics assembly design, argued as a fingerprint of deliberate synthetic genome construction -- which would entail the furin cleavage site being engineered rather than natural.
n112 [evidence] Rebuttal: fingerprint not statistically anomalous
    Multiple scientists responded that SARS-CoV-2's restriction-site pattern is not statistically anomalous: comparably even BsaI/BsmBI spacings occur in natural coronavirus genomes, the analysis depended on selective enzyme choice and modeling assumptions, and the preprint was not peer-reviewed -- so the pattern is consistent with a natural genome.
n113 [claim] Restriction pattern is not evidence of engineering
    The restriction-site 'fingerprint' does not indicate synthetic origin: comparably even BsaI/BsmBI patterns occur by chance in natural coronavirus genomes, so it is not evidence of engineering.
n114 [evidence] Rare CGG-CGG codon doublet at the FCS
    The two arginine residues of the SARS-CoV-2 furin cleavage site are encoded by a rare CGG-CGG codon doublet; the CGG codon is uncommon in coronaviruses (relative usage near 0 in pangolin CoV, ~0.08 in bat CoV, ~0.3 in SARS-CoV-2), which some have argued makes the doublet improbable in a naturally-arising SARSr-CoV.
n115 [claim] CGG-CGG doublet as an engineering signature
    The rare CGG-CGG codon doublet encoding the furin cleavage site's arginines is a possible signature of human codon optimization or deliberate insertion rather than natural evolution.
n116 [evidence] Rebuttal: CGG occurs naturally + is conserved
    Rebuttal (peer-reviewed): CGG occurs in SARS-CoV and SARS-CoV-2 at comparable frequencies and codon rarity cannot establish provenance; moreover both CGG codons are >99.8% conserved across millions of sequenced SARS-CoV-2 genomes, indicating natural functional constraint rather than an inserted low-fitness artifact that selection would have removed (PNAS 2022, 'the furin cleavage site was not engineered').
n117 [claim] CGG doublet is not evidence of engineering
    The CGG-CGG codon doublet is not evidence of engineering: comparable CGG usage occurs naturally in related coronaviruses and the codons' near-total conservation indicates natural functional selection, not human optimization.
n118 [evidence] Spike bound human ACE2 well from the start
    Lab-leak proponents have noted that SARS-CoV-2's spike bound human ACE2 efficiently in the earliest sampled genomes, arguing this unusually good fit at emergence is more consistent with pre-adaptation (e.g. via laboratory passage in humanized systems) than with a fresh zoonotic jump.
n119 [claim] Human-ACE2 pre-adaptation suggests lab origin
    SARS-CoV-2's efficient human-ACE2 binding at emergence indicates pre-adaptation to humans, argued as more consistent with a laboratory origin than a recent natural spillover.
n120 [evidence] RBD-ACE2 binding was sub-optimal at emergence
    Andersen et al. (2020) reported that the SARS-CoV-2 receptor-binding domain's interaction with human ACE2 was computationally predicted to be sub-optimal -- not the ideal an engineer would design -- and later work found early RBD mutations increased ACE2 affinity, so the spike was not pre-optimized at emergence.
n121 [claim] Sub-optimal binding indicates natural selection
    The spike was not pre-adapted or optimized for human ACE2 at emergence -- its binding was sub-optimal and improved by later mutation -- which is evidence of ongoing natural selection rather than laboratory pre-adaptation.
n122 [evidence] Early case-finding emphasized market links
    Early COVID-19 case-finding in Wuhan initially emphasized patients with a Huanan-market link (the market was publicly flagged as a suspected source in early January 2020), and several hospitals that received the earliest recognized cases are located near the market -- either of which could bias the apparent geographic clustering of early cases toward the market.
n123 [claim] Epidemiology points to a market origin
    The epidemiological evidence -- the earliest known cases and the SARS-CoV-2-positive environmental samples both cluster at the Huanan market, co-located with live-wildlife stalls and wildlife DNA -- points to a market origin of the outbreak.
n124 [claim] Phylogenetics is consistent with zoonotic origin
    The molecular-epidemiological and phylogenetic evidence (the lineage-A/B structure and the timing/pattern of early diversification) is most consistent with zoonotic introduction at the market.
n125 [claim] Genome shows natural evolution, no engineering
    The SARS-CoV-2 genome shows the marks of natural evolution and no signatures of deliberate engineering: no synthetic-assembly fingerprint survives scrutiny, and the receptor-binding domain was sub-optimal at emergence (consistent with selection, not design).
n126 [claim] Base rate favors zoonotic spillover
    The base rate strongly favors zoonosis: prior emerging human coronaviruses (SARS-CoV-1, MERS) arose overwhelmingly through natural spillover from an animal reservoir.
n127 [claim] Expert/IC review found no lab evidence
    Expert scientific bodies and dedicated intelligence investigations judged natural origin more likely and surfaced no positive evidence of a laboratory origin -- and the absence of an identified intermediate host is expected and not strong evidence against zoonosis.
n128 [claim] Two-intro result depends on excluding intermediate genomes
    Pekar et al.-s two-introduction result depended on excluding 20 intermediate-haplotype genomes, and critics argue that exclusion was not justified by sequence quality -- so an intermediate-genome (single-introduction) history is not ruled out.
n129 [claim] The two-introduction Bayes factor is a model artifact
    Reanalyses (notably McCowan 2025) argue the ~60 Bayes-factor support Pekar et al. reported for two introductions is an artifact of asymmetric model-acceptance conditions rather than robust evidence.
n130 [claim] The tree root cannot be confidently placed
    The root of the SARS-CoV-2 tree cannot be confidently placed (Bloom 2025): standard rooting principles conflict and lineages A and B differ by only two SNVs, so a single introduction with early diversification is not excluded.
n131 [claim] The FCS is consistent with natural origin
    The furin cleavage site is consistent with natural origin: its similarity to the human ENaC motif is only partial, several of the shared residues already occur in related coronaviruses, and the codons differ from the human gene -- so the site need not have been borrowed or engineered.
n132 [claim] The engineering-signature arguments are each rebutted
    The specific engineering-signature arguments for the furin site -- the deliberate-design logic, the restriction-site fingerprint, and the CGG-codon doublet -- have each been rebutted.
n133 [claim] Expert/IC review: probably not engineered
    Expert genomic analysis (the Proximal Origin paper) and the U.S. intelligence community assessed that SARS-CoV-2 was probably not deliberately genetically engineered.
