# Rating packet — debate-graph-v2, Round 6 (BLIND: no scores shown)

## Nodes (id [kind] title — text)

n001 [claim] Mapping won't improve debate
    A structured, collaborative argument-mapping platform will NOT meaningfully improve real-world reasoning or debate on contested questions.

n002 [claim] A faithful map adds value
    A faithful, collaborative argument map meaningfully improves collective reasoning on contested questions -- as a durable shared artifact for third parties, not a debate-winning tool for the arguers.

n003 [claim] Depends what you demand of it
    Whether an argument-mapping platform 'works' depends on what is demanded of it: it cannot RESOLVE genuinely value-laden disagreement, but it can faithfully MAP it -- so the dispute over such platforms is substantially about the GOAL, not the mechanism.

n004 [claim] Maps assume a shape arguments lack
    The argument-map form presumes a premise-to-conclusion skeleton with locatable false links -- a shape that real disagreement does not have.

n005 [claim] No clean premises to point at
    Real arguments almost never decompose into clean premises and a conclusion where a skeptic can point to the one false premise or invalid link.

n006 [claim] Rarely a false fact or fallacy
    Real disagreements rarely hinge on a checkable false fact or a nameable fallacy -- and when one is pointed out, people simply drop it and keep arguing on other grounds.

n007 [evidence] Expert panel: weighing, not facts
    When a panel of experts who disagreed sharply about AI risk tried to explain their disagreement, the closest anyone came was that some weight a theoretical intelligence-explosion argument heavily while others distrust theoretical arguments generally -- no false fact and no named fallacy, just different weighing.

n008 [claim] More detail makes maps worse
    Adding more premises and links to an argument map makes it worse, not better: it recasts inherently probabilistic reasoning as logically necessary syllogisms and trains readers to hunt for a single link to refute.

n009 [claim] Maps settle the wrong thing
    Even a completed argument map settles only the truth of atomic premises, which is not where real disagreements are decided.

n010 [claim] The real work is weighing
    The load-bearing work in a real dispute is quantifying magnitudes and weighing them against each other.

n011 [evidence] The lockdown weighing example
    A published worked example: even granting 'lockdowns hurt the economy' as true, concluding 'lockdowns were bad' still requires quantifying the economic harm, all the other harms, the benefits, comparing them for a specific proposal, and a theory of interpersonal utility.

n012 [claim] Values generate the disagreement
    The deepest disagreements are generated by differing values and ethical frameworks (e.g. 'this policy violates rights regardless of any cost-benefit'), which no amount of factual mapping can resolve.

n013 [claim] Positions are over-determined
    People hold positions on contested questions through many weakly-held considerations, not one decisive crux.

n014 [claim] Flipping one point moves no one
    Refuting any single consideration rarely changes anyone's overall position.

n015 [claim] Wrong altitude
    The decisive action in a disagreement happens at the level of worldviews and other high-level generators; surface-level claims -- where argument maps operate -- are the wrong altitude.

n016 [claim] Not viable in practice
    Argument-mapping is not a practically viable route to improving real-world debate.

n017 [claim] Inefficient at changing minds
    Formal argument-mapping is an inefficient way to change anyone's mind in a live dispute.

n018 [claim] People don't want rigor
    Almost no one actually wants to argue more rigorously: people who argue about contested questions are there to win and to express, not to update.

n020 [claim] The concessions cut against it
    The conceded limitations of argument-mapping platforms -- granted even by their builders -- bear directly against the claim that such platforms work.

n021 [claim] No consensus is produced
    If 'working' means making the disagreeing parties agree, argument maps do not work: they produce a shared map, not consensus.

n022 [claim] Internal ratings reward crowd bias
    A collaborative platform's internal rating rules can only measure agreement with its own crowd, so on a biased majority they reward the bias.

n023 [claim] Anchors are scarce where needed most
    Correcting a biased crowd requires external ground truth, which is scarcest on exactly the most contested questions.

n024 [evidence] Self-retired rating rules
    The assessment research behind one argument-mapping project publicly retired its own agreement-based reputation rule for burying the sharpest raters, found its discrimination scoring inverted on a biased crowd, and found its calibration had to decline when external anchors did not span the scale.

n025 [claim] Dispersion is unreliable
    Statistical dispersion in ratings is a low-reliability signal of where a map is genuinely contested, so contestedness has to be read from the map's structure instead.

n026 [claim] Human adoption unproven
    Whether real human communities will build, adopt, or trust collaborative argument maps is unproven; the demonstrations so far come from AI-agent panels.

n027 [claim] A richer grammar fits real argument
    An argument map does not have to assume the clean logical form the critique attacks: a grammar of graded support, rival positive claims, and joint grounds captures how real arguments actually behave.

n028 [claim] Graded support, not syllogisms
    An argument map need not force premise-to-conclusion form: support can be graded rather than binary, opposition can be a rival positive claim rather than a negation, and grounds can be marked as supporting only jointly.

n029 [claim] Many grounds are native
    A graph holds an over-determined position natively: a claim can carry many independent grounds with support apportioned across them, so no single decisive crux is required.

n030 [claim] Jettison-and-continue is modeled
    In a graded, support-only map, a refuted ground is demoted while its dependent survives on its remaining grounds -- 'drop the refuted point and continue' is the modeled normal case, not a failure of the form.

n031 [claim] Locate the crux, don't resolve it
    The proper job of a map on the hard parts of a dispute -- the weighing and the values -- is to locate and exhibit them, not to resolve them.

n032 [claim] Locate the weighing
    The goal of mapping a dispute is not to resolve the weighing but to LOCATE it: to make explicit where the weight sits and how wide the reasonable range is.

n033 [evidence] A contested map said 'unresolved'
    On a mapped and blind-panel-assessed build of a genuinely contested scientific question (the origin of a pandemic virus), the honest verdict node 'the causal question is currently unresolved' scored high -- the assessed map declined to manufacture a false resolution.

n034 [claim] Values get a first-class home
    Value claims can be represented first-class and separately: typed as prescriptions, barred from grounding factual claims, and rated on endorsement rather than truth -- so value-cruxes appear on the map AS values rather than being smuggled into factual claims.

n035 [claim] A viable route and audience exist
    Argument maps have a viable audience and production route that does not depend on changing the arguers' minds.

n036 [claim] Built for readers, not arguers
    An argument map's audience is third parties -- readers and future deciders consulting a durable shared artifact -- not the two arguers in a live dispute; changing the arguers' minds is the wrong success measure.

n037 [claim] AI authorship changes the economics
    AI agents can build, maintain, and rate argument maps at scale, removing the authorship cost that sank earlier mapping efforts.

n038 [evidence] Three questions built end-to-end
    Three differently-shaped contested questions -- a pandemic virus's origin, collider black-hole safety, and the healthfulness of eggs -- have each been built and blind-panel-assessed end-to-end into argument graphs by AI agents at low cost.

n039 [claim] The conditions have changed
    The inference from 'unsolved for two thousand years' to 'unsolvable' fails, because the enabling conditions for argument-mapping are genuinely new.

n040 [claim] New enabling conditions
    'Unsolved for two thousand years' does not imply unsolvable now: cheap AI authorship and rating, reputation-weighting, and enforced-blind assessment are conditions no previous attempt had.

n041 [claim] Precedents awaited their medium
    Encyclopedias, prediction markets, and systematic review each languished until the right medium and incentive structure arrived -- longstanding failure before a workable form is the norm for such tools, not evidence of impossibility.

n042 [claim] A dispute about the goal
    The strongest critiques of argument-mapping target RESOLUTION -- making the parties agree -- which mappers concede they do not deliver, while the mapping claim is about REPRESENTATION; the two sides are arguing about what the tool is FOR, not about whether its mechanism functions.

n043 [claim] This dispute maps, not resolves
    The dispute over whether argument-mapping 'works' is itself over-determined and partly value-laden, so by the critique's own analysis it will not be resolved -- it can only be located and mapped.

n044 [ought] A faithful map is worth it
    A tool that faithfully maps a disagreement -- making its value-cruxes explicit and openly unresolved -- is worth building, even if it never makes the parties agree.

n045 [ought] Only mind-changing counts
    A debate tool is only worth building if it actually changes minds or produces agreement; a map that leaves everyone where they started is not worth the effort.

n046 [claim] Change comes through the audience
    A public argument map improves collective understanding mainly through people who have not yet taken a side: new readers form their views in a world where weak arguments are already visibly weak, even if committed arguers never change their minds.

n047 [claim] The Wikipedia precedent
    Nearly all of Wikipedia's facts existed in print before Wikipedia; what ended casual factual disputes was easy, universal access to one trusted repository, which made checkable falsehoods embarrassing to repeat.

n050 [claim] No one holds the whole map
    No one can hold a complete map of a complicated dispute in their head -- every claim, every connection, and an accurate sense of how strongly each point is really supported -- so an explicit map adds information even for the most capable readers.

n051 [evidence] Memory limits bind even elite debate
    After the 2024 Rootclaim debate on COVID origins, the losing side said the live format gave a major advantage to the debater who had memorized more -- memory limits shaping the outcome even of an elite, high-stakes debate.

n052 [claim] A limit shared by every format
    The gap between what most people find convincing and what is true is a limit on every way of arguing -- essays, expert panels, peer review -- not a special defect of argument maps; a format can at best measure that gap and manage it.

n055 [claim] Explicit beats implicit
    A faithful map of a complicated dispute cannot be simpler than the dispute itself; the real choice is between an explicit map that can be shared, checked, and read a piece at a time, and an implicit map hidden in one person's head.

n057 [claim] Point-droppers supply coverage
    People who will not sustain a full argument still reliably contribute single points -- a correction, a source, a single objection -- which a collaborative map can collect and organize into coverage.

n059 [claim] Maps are hard to take in
    A complete argument map of a complicated dispute is genuinely hard for a person to take in at once.

n060 [claim] Maps tempt single-link over-defeat
    Heavy reliance on argument maps can tempt readers to treat one weak or refuted link as defeating an otherwise over-determined argument.

n061 [claim] Persistent failure is evidence of difficulty
    Across two millennia, no approach -- philosophy, rhetoric, or formal logic -- has 'solved debate'; that persistent failure is genuine evidence that improving reasoning at this scale is very hard, even if it does not prove a new tool cannot help.

n062 [claim] The points already exist, unassessed
    The points made in public arguments already exist -- scattered across essays, studies, and threads -- with no single trusted place where their strength has been fairly assessed.

n063 [claim] A public record makes weak arguments costly
    A public, trusted record of which arguments are known to be weak makes those arguments costly to repeat.

n064 [claim] Public reasoning quality rises over time
    As weak arguments grow costly to repeat, the overall quality of public reasoning rises over time.

n065 [claim] Trusted essayists show a fair survey's value
    The most trusted essayists show that a comprehensive, fair survey of a hard question is valuable; a collaborative map aims to make that kind of survey available broadly, without depending on the rare writer able and willing to produce one.

n066 [claim] Partisans defend a visibly losing side
    Partisans reliably arrive to defend their side when it is visibly losing, supplying real counter-arguments without anyone needing to sustain a rigorous debate.
