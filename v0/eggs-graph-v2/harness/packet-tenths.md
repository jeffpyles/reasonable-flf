## Nodes (id [kind] title — phrasings)

n001 [claim] Eggs meaningfully raise CVD and mortality risk
    Egg and dietary-cholesterol consumption meaningfully raises cardiovascular-disease and mortality risk for most people.

n002 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's04', 'title': 'Association between Egg Consumption and Cholesterol Concentration: meta-analysis of RCTs (PMC; PubMed 32635569)', 'url': 'https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7400894/'}] RCT meta-analysis: egg effect on TC/LDL/HDL (s04)
    A meta-analysis of 28 randomized controlled trials found egg consumption raised total cholesterol by about 5.6 mg/dL, LDL-C by about 5.55 mg/dL, and HDL-C by about 2.13 mg/dL relative to low-egg control diets.

n003 [claim] Modest LDL rise from eggs (RCT evidence)
    Egg consumption raises LDL cholesterol only modestly in randomized-controlled-trial evidence.

n004 [claim] Eggs raise HDL too - ratios roughly unchanged
    Egg consumption raises HDL cholesterol alongside LDL, leaving cholesterol ratios (LDL:HDL, TC:HDL) roughly unchanged.

n005 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's05', 'title': 'Impact of dietary cholesterol from eggs and saturated fat on LDL cholesterol: randomized cross-over study (Am J Clin Nutr, 2025)', 'url': 'https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(25)00253-9/abstract'}] 2025 crossover RCT: eggs did not raise LDL-C (s05)
    A 2025 randomized cross-over trial (61 adults, three 5-week isocaloric diets) found that a high-cholesterol diet including 2 eggs/day did not raise LDL-C relative to control, and slightly lowered it.

n006 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's05', 'title': 'Impact of dietary cholesterol from eggs and saturated fat on LDL cholesterol: randomized cross-over study (Am J Clin Nutr, 2025)', 'url': 'https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(25)00253-9/abstract'}] 2025 crossover RCT: sat fat, not cholesterol, tracked LDL-C (s05)
    Across the diets in the same 2025 randomized cross-over trial, dietary saturated-fat intake was significantly related to LDL-C, but dietary-cholesterol intake was not.

n007 [claim] Saturated fat, not dietary cholesterol, drives LDL (2025 crux)
    Saturated fat, not the cholesterol in eggs, is the main driver of LDL-cholesterol elevation.

n008 [claim] Dietary cholesterol meaningfully raises LDL
    Dietary cholesterol meaningfully raises serum LDL cholesterol for most people.

n009 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's13', 'title': 'Dietary Cholesterol and the Lack of Evidence in Cardiovascular Disease (review, PMC)', 'url': 'https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6024687/'}] Review: dietary-cholesterol-CVD evidence is weak (s13)
    A review argues the evidence linking dietary cholesterol to cardiovascular disease is weak, because dietary cholesterol has a limited and variable effect on serum cholesterol.

n010 [claim] Dietary cholesterol's effect on serum cholesterol is limited/variable
    Dietary cholesterol has a limited and variable effect on serum cholesterol for most people.

n011 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's06', 'title': 'Eggs do not raise blood cholesterol: results of a randomised controlled trial (Proc Nutr Soc)', 'url': 'https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/proceedings-of-the-nutrition-society/article/eggs-do-not-raise-blood-cholesterol-results-of-a-randomised-controlled-trial/47EF5DA93D2B84431CC1BC0BBD504DFF'}] RCT: adding eggs did not significantly raise blood cholesterol (s06)
    A randomized controlled trial reported that adding eggs to the diet did not significantly raise blood cholesterol.

n012 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's13', 'title': 'Dietary Cholesterol and the Lack of Evidence in Cardiovascular Disease (review, PMC)', 'url': 'https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6024687/'}] Hyper- vs hypo-responder variation (s13)
    A review notes individuals differ in how strongly dietary cholesterol raises serum cholesterol ('hyper-responders' vs 'hypo-responders'), complicating population-wide dietary advice.

n013 [claim] Hyper-/hypo-responder variation complicates universal advice
    Individual variation in cholesterol response ('hyper-responder' vs 'hypo-responder') means a single population-wide dietary-cholesterol recommendation does not fit everyone.

n014 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's05', 'title': 'Impact of dietary cholesterol from eggs and saturated fat on LDL cholesterol: randomized cross-over study (Am J Clin Nutr, 2025)', 'url': 'https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(25)00253-9/abstract'}] 2025 crossover RCT measures surrogate lipid endpoints, not CV events (s05)
    A 2025 randomized cross-over trial measuring egg and saturated-fat effects on LDL-C is a small, short-term trial on surrogate lipid endpoints, not on cardiovascular events.

n015 [claim] Lipid RCT endpoints are surrogates, not hard CV outcomes
    Blood-lipid measurements such as LDL, HDL, and total cholesterol in RCTs are surrogate markers, not direct measurements of cardiovascular events or mortality.

n016 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's09', 'title': 'Egg consumption and risk of CVD: three large prospective US cohorts + updated meta-analysis (BMJ 2020)', 'url': 'https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7190072/'}] US cohorts + meta: no CVD link up to ~1 egg/day
    Three large US prospective cohorts plus an updated dose-response meta-analysis found that moderate egg consumption, up to about one egg per day, was not associated with cardiovascular-disease risk in the general population.

n017 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's03', 'title': 'Eggs and Cardiovascular Disease Risk: An Update of Recent Evidence (review, PMC)', 'url': 'https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10285014/'}] Review: no clear general-population CVD link
    A recent review of the egg-consumption literature concludes that for the general population, egg consumption is not clearly associated with cardiovascular-disease risk.

n018 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's03', 'title': 'Eggs and Cardiovascular Disease Risk: An Update of Recent Evidence (review, PMC)', 'url': 'https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10285014/'}] Review: evidence base mixed, mostly observational
    The same review notes the overall evidence base on egg consumption and cardiovascular disease is mixed and dominated by observational studies of modest effect size.

n019 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's02', 'title': 'Egg and Dietary Cholesterol Intake and Risk of All-Cause, CVD, and Cancer Mortality: dose-response meta-analysis (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2022)', 'url': 'https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.878979/full'}] Dose-response meta: +7%/day all-cause mortality
    A dose-response meta-analysis pooling about 55 studies (~2.7 million individuals) found each additional egg per day associated with about 7% higher all-cause mortality and about 13% higher cancer mortality.

n020 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's02', 'title': 'Egg and Dietary Cholesterol Intake and Risk of All-Cause, CVD, and Cancer Mortality: dose-response meta-analysis (Frontiers in Nutrition, 2022)', 'url': 'https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.878979/full'}] Same meta-analysis: no CVD-mortality signal
    In the same dose-response meta-analysis, egg consumption was not significantly associated with cardiovascular-disease mortality specifically.

n021 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's01', 'title': 'Zhao et al., Dietary Cholesterol, Serum Cholesterol, and Egg Consumption With Overall and Cause-Specific Mortality (Circulation, updated meta-analysis)', 'url': 'https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.121.057642'}] 41-cohort meta: higher overall + CVD mortality
    An updated meta-analysis of about 41 prospective cohort studies found higher dietary-cholesterol and egg consumption associated with increased overall and cardiovascular-disease mortality.

n022 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's01', 'title': 'Zhao et al., Dietary Cholesterol, Serum Cholesterol, and Egg Consumption With Overall and Cause-Specific Mortality (Circulation, updated meta-analysis)', 'url': 'https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.121.057642'}] Geographic heterogeneity: US positive, Asia null
    In the same updated meta-analysis, the egg-cardiovascular-disease association was geographically heterogeneous: a significant positive association in US cohorts, a marginal association in European cohorts, and no association in Asian cohorts.

n023 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's01', 'title': 'Zhao et al., Dietary Cholesterol, Serum Cholesterol, and Egg Consumption With Overall and Cause-Specific Mortality (Circulation, updated meta-analysis)', 'url': 'https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.121.057642'}] Cholesterol dose-response: all-cause up, CVD unclear
    In the same updated meta-analysis, each 100 mg/day increment in dietary cholesterol was associated with higher all-cause and cancer mortality but not clearly with cardiovascular-disease mortality.

n024 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's10', 'title': 'Egg and cholesterol consumption and mortality in the US: population-based cohort study (PLOS Medicine, 2021)', 'url': 'https://journals.plos.org/plosmedicine/article?id=10.1371/journal.pmed.1003508'}] US cohort: mortality link driven by cholesterol content
    A large US population-based cohort study found higher egg and dietary-cholesterol intake associated with higher all-cause and cardiovascular-disease mortality, with the association driven substantially by cholesterol content rather than eggs per se.

n025 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's10', 'title': 'Egg and cholesterol consumption and mortality in the US: population-based cohort study (PLOS Medicine, 2021)', 'url': 'https://journals.plos.org/plosmedicine/article?id=10.1371/journal.pmed.1003508'}] Egg-white substitution associated with lower mortality
    In the same cohort study, statistically substituting egg whites or other protein sources for whole eggs was associated with lower mortality.

n026 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's07', 'title': 'Longitudinal association between egg consumption and CVD risk: interaction with type 2 diabetes (Nutrition & Diabetes, 2018)', 'url': 'https://www.nature.com/articles/s41387-018-0033-1'}] T2D modifies egg-CVD association
    A longitudinal cohort study found that egg consumption's association with cardiovascular disease is modified by type-2-diabetes status: elevated cardiovascular-disease risk appears among people with diabetes at higher egg intake, but not clearly in the general population.

n027 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's07', 'title': 'Longitudinal association between egg consumption and CVD risk: interaction with type 2 diabetes (Nutrition & Diabetes, 2018)', 'url': 'https://www.nature.com/articles/s41387-018-0033-1'}] Diabetes-subgroup signal called relatively consistent
    The same study characterizes the diabetes-subgroup interaction as one of the more consistent signals in the egg-consumption literature.

n028 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's08', 'title': 'Egg consumption in relation to risk of CVD and diabetes: systematic review and meta-analysis (PMC)', 'url': 'https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3683816/'}] Meta-analysis: null in general population, positive in diabetes
    A systematic review and meta-analysis found no overall egg-cardiovascular-disease association in the general population but an increased cardiovascular-disease risk among people with diabetes eating more eggs.

n029 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's08', 'title': 'Egg consumption in relation to risk of CVD and diabetes: systematic review and meta-analysis (PMC)', 'url': 'https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3683816/'}] T2D + >7 eggs/week: ~69% more CVD events
    In the same review, pooled estimates indicate that people with type-2 diabetes eating more than about seven eggs per week have substantially more cardiovascular-disease events, on the order of 69% more, than those eating few eggs.

n030 [claim] General population: no meaningful CVD-risk increase up to ~1/day
    For the general population, egg consumption up to about one egg per day is not associated with meaningfully increased cardiovascular-disease risk.

n031 [claim] Mortality signal is mainly all-cause/cancer, not CVD-specific
    The mortality association found for egg or dietary-cholesterol intake in several cohort meta-analyses is driven mainly by all-cause and cancer mortality, not by cardiovascular-disease-specific mortality.

n032 [claim] Some meta-analyses find a CVD-specific mortality signal
    Egg or dietary-cholesterol consumption is associated with increased cardiovascular-disease-specific mortality in some updated cohort meta-analyses, not only all-cause mortality.

n033 [claim] Mortality signal is a real causal effect of eggs
    The small increase in all-cause mortality associated with higher egg intake reflects a real causal effect of egg or dietary-cholesterol consumption.

n034 [claim] Mortality signal reflects residual confounding, not eggs
    The small increase in all-cause mortality associated with higher egg intake reflects residual confounding, for example by dietary pattern or other lifestyle factors correlated with egg consumption, rather than a causal effect of eggs.

n035 [claim] Geographic heterogeneity points to confounding
    The geographic heterogeneity of the egg-cardiovascular/mortality association (positive in US cohorts, marginal in European cohorts, null in Asian cohorts) is evidence that the association reflects confounding by dietary pattern or lifestyle rather than a direct causal effect of eggs.

n036 [claim] Heterogeneity may reflect real dose/susceptibility differences
    The geographic heterogeneity in the egg-cardiovascular/mortality association may instead reflect real differences in egg-consumption dose, food preparation, or population susceptibility across regions, rather than purely confounding.

n037 [claim] Diabetes subgroup: elevated CVD risk at higher egg intake
    Among people with type-2 diabetes, higher egg intake, more than about seven eggs per week, is associated with meaningfully elevated cardiovascular-disease risk, even though this pattern is not clearly present in the general population.

n038 [claim] Diabetes-subgroup risk is a real causal effect of eggs
    The elevated cardiovascular-disease risk seen with higher egg intake in the type-2-diabetes subgroup reflects a real causal effect of eggs or dietary cholesterol on cardiovascular risk in that population.

n039 [claim] Diabetes-subgroup risk reflects confounding, not eggs
    The elevated cardiovascular-disease risk seen with higher egg intake in the type-2-diabetes subgroup reflects confounding by other diabetes-related dietary or lifestyle factors rather than a causal effect of eggs.

n040 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's16', 'title': 'Egg consumption and risk of chronic disease: an (un-)resolved issue? (Am J Clin Nutr editorial/review)', 'url': 'https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(22)01058-9/fulltext'}] Egg-disease evidence base: mixed observational findings (s16)
    Prospective observational evidence on egg or dietary-cholesterol consumption and chronic-disease outcomes is mixed, with increased, decreased, and null associations all reported across studies.

n041 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's16', 'title': 'Egg consumption and risk of chronic disease: an (un-)resolved issue? (Am J Clin Nutr editorial/review)', 'url': 'https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(22)01058-9/fulltext'}] No long-term RCTs on hard endpoints exist (s16)
    No long-term randomized controlled trials of egg consumption on hard clinical endpoints -- cardiovascular events or all-cause/cardiovascular mortality -- exist.

n042 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's16', 'title': 'Egg consumption and risk of chronic disease: an (un-)resolved issue? (Am J Clin Nutr editorial/review)', 'url': 'https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(22)01058-9/fulltext'}] Residual confounding limits cohort estimates (s16)
    Prospective cohort estimates of the egg-consumption/chronic-disease association are limited by residual confounding -- healthy-user bias, socioeconomic factors, and broader dietary-pattern factors -- that observational statistical adjustment cannot fully remove.

n043 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's16', 'title': 'Egg consumption and risk of chronic disease: an (un-)resolved issue? (Am J Clin Nutr editorial/review)', 'url': 'https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(22)01058-9/fulltext'}] Long-term hard-endpoint RCT on eggs is impractical (s16)
    A long-term double-blind randomized controlled trial of egg consumption on cardiovascular events is impractical to conduct, so the field must rely on imperfect observational evidence instead.

n044 [claim] Observational association does not establish causation
    Because egg-consumption studies are predominantly observational, an association between egg or dietary-cholesterol intake and a health outcome does not by itself establish that egg or dietary-cholesterol consumption causes that outcome.

n045 [claim] Cohort associations may reflect unremovable residual confounding
    Residual confounding that observational statistical adjustment cannot fully remove means cohort-observed associations between egg consumption and cardiovascular-disease or mortality outcomes may partly or wholly reflect confounding rather than a causal effect of eggs.

n046 [claim] Surrogate-endpoint RCT evidence does not settle clinical-endpoint effects
    Randomized-controlled-trial evidence that eggs modestly affect LDL cholesterol, a surrogate marker, does not by itself establish eggs' effect on cardiovascular events or mortality, the clinical endpoints the question is actually about.

n047 [claim] The causal question remains genuinely unresolved
    Whether egg or dietary-cholesterol consumption causally raises cardiovascular-disease and mortality risk for most people remains genuinely unresolved by the current evidence base.

n048 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's11', 'title': 'Final 2015-2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans (Health Affairs brief)', 'url': 'https://www.healthaffairs.org/do/10.1377/hpb20160331.683121/'}] 2015 guidelines drop the 300mg cholesterol cap
    The 2015-2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans removed the prior recommendation to limit dietary cholesterol to under 300 mg/day.

n049 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's11', 'title': 'Final 2015-2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans (Health Affairs brief)', 'url': 'https://www.healthaffairs.org/do/10.1377/hpb20160331.683121/'}] Committee: cholesterol 'not a nutrient of concern'
    The Dietary Guidelines Advisory Committee for the 2015-2020 guidelines stated that cholesterol is 'not a nutrient of concern for overconsumption,' citing a lack of clear evidence for the specific 300 mg/day cap.

n050 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's11', 'title': 'Final 2015-2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans (Health Affairs brief)', 'url': 'https://www.healthaffairs.org/do/10.1377/hpb20160331.683121/'}] Guidelines retained 'as low as practical' cholesterol advice
    The 2015-2020 Dietary Guidelines nonetheless advised keeping dietary cholesterol intake as low as practical, even after removing the numeric cap.

n051 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's12', 'title': 'Harvard Nutrition Source commentary on the 2015 Dietary Guidelines', 'url': 'https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/2016/01/07/new-dietary-guidelines-remove-restriction-on-total-fat-and-set-limit-for-added-sugars-but-censor-conclusions/'}] Commentary: cap removal reflects genuine uncertainty
    Nutrition commentary on the 2015 Dietary Guidelines noted that removing the numeric cholesterol cap while retaining softer cholesterol advice reflected genuine scientific uncertainty about a specific numeric limit.

n052 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's12', 'title': 'Harvard Nutrition Source commentary on the 2015 Dietary Guidelines', 'url': 'https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/2016/01/07/new-dietary-guidelines-remove-restriction-on-total-fat-and-set-limit-for-added-sugars-but-censor-conclusions/'}] 300mg cap's mid-20th-century origin, not a trial
    The original 300 mg/day dietary-cholesterol cap originated from mid-20th-century guidance (AHA 1968; U.S. Dietary Goals 1977) rather than from a trial establishing that specific threshold.

n053 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's14', 'title': 'Egg Consumption in US Children Associated With Greater Daily Nutrient Intakes (PMC)', 'url': 'https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6566164/'}] Egg consumption linked to higher nutrient intake (children's survey)
    A US children's nutrition-survey study found egg consumption associated with greater daily intake of protein, lutein and zeaxanthin, choline, alpha-linolenic acid, and DHA, without displacing other nutrients from the diet.

n054 [evidence] [source: {'ref': 's14', 'title': 'Egg Consumption in US Children Associated With Greater Daily Nutrient Intakes (PMC)', 'url': 'https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6566164/'}] Eggs as a concentrated choline source
    Eggs are among the few concentrated dietary sources of choline, a nutrient many people under-consume.

n057 [claim] Eggs are nutrient-dense
    Eggs are a nutrient-dense food, supplying complete protein and concentrated amounts of several under-consumed nutrients including choline, lutein, and zeaxanthin.

n058 [claim] Eggs support weight management via protein/satiety
    Eggs' high protein content and satiety help support effective weight management.

n059 [claim] Lutein/zeaxanthin and reduced eye-disease risk
    Lutein and zeaxanthin from egg yolks are associated with reduced risk of cataracts and age-related macular degeneration.

n060 [claim] Cap removal = evidence-based reassessment
    The 2015-2020 removal of the 300 mg/day dietary-cholesterol cap reflected an evidence-based reassessment that dietary cholesterol is not a population-level nutrient of concern.

n061 [claim] Cap removal left genuine scientific caution, not exoneration
    The 2015-2020 guidelines' retained advice to keep dietary cholesterol intake 'as low as practical' despite removing the numeric cap shows the science on dietary cholesterol was not fully settled, not a clean exoneration.

n062 [ought] Eating eggs is reasonable for most people
    For most people, eating eggs in moderation is a reasonable dietary choice.

n063 [ought] Precaution is warranted for most people
    A precautionary approach to egg and dietary-cholesterol intake is warranted for most people.

n064 [claim] Eggs do not meaningfully raise CVD risk
    Egg and dietary-cholesterol consumption does not meaningfully raise cardiovascular-disease risk for most people.

n065 [claim] Eggs provide real nutritional benefits
    Eggs provide real nutritional benefits -- complete protein, choline, lutein and zeaxanthin, and satiety that supports weight management.

n066 [claim] No trial evidence on real clinical endpoints
    The trial evidence that could settle egg causation on hard clinical endpoints is absent or measures only surrogate markers.

n067 [claim] Observational evidence can't pin down causation
    The available observational and subgroup evidence cannot establish whether eggs causally affect health outcomes for most people.

    A food with real nutritional benefits and only modest, unproven risks is a reasonable thing to eat.

    When a plausible health harm is genuinely unresolved, limiting exposure is prudent, especially for higher-risk groups.
